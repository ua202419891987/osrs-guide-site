💥 Why Naval Cannonballs Are So Expensive

Before you can fix the problem, you need to understand it. Naval cannonballs are the ammunition for your ship's cannons — every volley you fire during a naval engagement consumes a stack. Unlike most skilling supplies, cannonballs have a perfect storm of economic pressures that keep their price high:

① Universal Demand, Capped Supply

Every sailor who wants to do naval combat — and that is the majority of players once they unlock cannons — needs cannonballs. There is no free alternative ammo for ship-to-ship fighting. On the supply side, cannonballs are made through Smithing, and the smithing process is slow, click-intensive, and low-XP relative to its GP value. Very few players mass-produce them purely to sell, so supply stays thin while demand is near-infinite.

② The "Cannon Sink" Keeps GE Prices Sticky

Regular Dwarf Multicannon owners already consume millions of cannonballs per day across the game. Sailing's naval cannons plug directly into that same demand pool. Because the Old School cannonball price is propped up by decades of PvM cannon usage, the Sailing ammo variant inherits that price floor. When Sailing launched, naval-grade balls simply tracked the existing cannonball economy upward.

③ Crafting Cost & Material Scarcity

Naval cannonballs require steel bars (or higher-tier bars) plus a smithing step, and the higher tiers (round shot, grapeshot, chain shot) need rarer inputs that only drop from Sailing content itself. That creates a feedback loop: you need to already be doing Sailing content to get the materials to make the ammo to do more Sailing content. Early adopters paid a heavy premium before the material loop matured.

💡 The Core Insight

Cannonballs are expensive because they are a forced consumption item with no substitute. The moment you stop firing, you stop spending — so the entire strategy of "beating the cost" is about firing less, not earning more (though earning more helps too).

Run one profitable Sailing method for 20–30 minutes, then spend that GP on a cannonball stockpile and burn it on naval combat you actually enjoy. You never touch your main bank, and the ammo "cost" becomes invisible because it's paid for by the same skill. This is how most advanced sailors afford unlimited naval PvP.

🛡️ Cheaper Strategies — Cut Your Cannonball Burn

You don't need more GP if you simply fire fewer balls. These techniques cut ammo use by 30–70% with no loss in results:

① Use the Right Shot Tier for the Job

Round shot is your bread-and-butter. Save expensive grapeshot (anti-crew) and chain shot (rigging damage) for when they actually matter. Firing chain shot at a lone trader is pure waste. Match the shell to the target.

② Fire Only on Locked Targets

Unlocked volleys miss or glance. Wait for the targeting reticle to lock (or the "In Range" prompt) before pressing fire. Disciplined sailors report 40%+ fewer balls per kill just from not wasting shots on strafing passes.

③ Repair Your Cannons

Damaged cannons have reduced accuracy and waste ammo on weak hits. A quick repair between voyages keeps every ball landing. The repair cost is a fraction of the ammo you'd lose to a degraded battery.

④ Lead With Cheaper Activities, Save Naval for Loot

Use trade routes and salvaging for steady income, and reserve cannon-firing for naval bosses and PvP where the loot justifies the burn. Treat casual patrols as optional, not mandatory.

💡 Bulk-Buy When Prices Dip

Cannonball prices swing with patch notes and content drops. Buy a few hundred K worth during dips and you effectively "lock in" cheap ammo for weeks. Never buy at the post-update spike if you can wait 48 hours.

⚖️ Is Naval Combat Actually Worth the Cost?

Short answer: it depends entirely on what you want from Sailing.

① Worth It If…

  • You enjoy PvP or ship duels — there is no substitute for cannons here.
  • You're chasing naval boss loot — net GP/hr stays strong (see table above).
  • You run valuable trade routes — escorting protects cargo worth far more than the ammo.
  • You treat Sailing as endgame content — the ammo is the "entry fee" to the best part.

② Not Worth It If…

  • Your only goal is GP/hr — trade routes and salvaging beat casual patrols.
  • You're below ~73 Sailing — you lack the efficient money methods to fund ammo comfortably.
  • You hate click-intensive ammo management — the tracking and restocking is a chore.

📈 The Verdict

Naval combat is worth the cost for engagement and high-tier loot, not for efficient GP. If you want money, let Sailing pay for itself through trading and salvaging, then spend the surplus on cannons for fun. Don't force naval grinding to "make it profitable" — that's where the frustration (and the GP drain) comes from.

🔄 Low-Cost Alternatives to Naval Cannon Combat

If the ammo bill is killing your motivation, pivot to these Sailing activities that barely touch your cannonball stack:

① Trade Routes (No Ammo Required)

Buy low at one port, sail to another, sell high. Zero cannonballs consumed, predictable 350K–1.5M GP/hr, and it directly improves your ship handling for when you do fight. The single best "I'm broke but want Sailing XP and GP" activity.

② Shipwreck Salvaging

Salvaging wrecks earns GP and materials (some of which craft into cannonballs, closing the loop). Standard salvaging is viable from 15 Sailing; mercenary wrecks at 73 Sailing pay ~330K+/hr with no ammo spend.

③ Passive & Non-Combat Sailing

Cargo contracts, courier runs, and exploration give XP and income with little to no ammo use. They're slower than bossing but cost nothing to run, making them ideal "funding" activities between naval sessions.

④ Buy Ammo With Alternative Income

If Sailing isn't your thing yet, earn cannonballs through any high-GP method (Slayer, bossing, herb runs) and convert that GP at the GE. The ammo is just GP in another shape — fund it however you make money.

A drained GP stack makes you a target for scams promising "free cannonball supplies" or "ammo doublers." Never trade with unofficial bots or click suspicious links. Our Account Security Guide covers the live scams going around right now.

❓ FAQ — Naval Cannonball Costs

Why did naval cannonball prices spike right after Sailing launched?

Launch created a massive new demand source that pulled from the existing cannonball economy. Thin smithing supply couldn't keep up instantly, so prices jumped. They stabilize over time as more players mass-produce ammo and material loops mature — but the underlying price floor from PvM cannon use keeps them from ever being "cheap."

What's the cheapest way to keep a naval cannon fed?

The "ammo loop": run Deep Sea Trawling or Shipwreck Salvaging for 20–30 minutes, convert the GP to cannonballs, then fight. You never touch your main bank, and the most expensive shot tiers become affordable because the same skill pays for them. Bulk-buying during GE price dips compounds the savings.

Can I do Sailing profitably without ever firing a cannon?

Yes. Trade routes, salvage wrecks, cargo contracts, and courier runs all earn GP and XP with little to no ammo use. Many players reach high Sailing entirely through non-combat methods and only pick up cannons later for PvP or boss loot.

Is naval PvP worth the cannonball cost?

Only if you enjoy the mode. Loot from sunk players is variable and the ammo burn is the highest in the game (400K–1M+ GP/hr per side). Treat PvP as entertainment with a cost, not as a money method — unless you're consistently winning fights and looting fitted ships.

Do higher-tier cannonballs (grapeshot, chain shot) cost that much more?

Yes — they need rarer Sailing-dropped materials, so they can cost several times more per shot than round shot. Use them only when the effect matters (boarding crews, disabling rigging). For plain damage, round shot is the economical default.
