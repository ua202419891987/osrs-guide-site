⚔️ 1. Windrose Combat Philosophy — Aggressive But Patient

Windrose's combat is a deliberate hybrid. On land, it plays like a faster, more forgiving soulslike — weapons have weight, attacks have recovery, and enemies hit hard. But unlike Dark Souls, Windrose rewards aggression. The longer you play defensively, the more enemies you attract, and group fights are exponentially harder than 1v1 duels. The philosophy: isolate one enemy, destroy them quickly (ideally in 1-2 combo chains), and move to the next before reinforcements arrive. Hesitation is death.

At sea, combat transforms into a tactical chess match of positioning, wind management, and ammunition selection. You are simultaneously the captain, gunner, and helmsman of your vessel. Broadside cannons deal the most damage but require you to expose your ship's flank. Bow chasers let you fire while pursuing. Stern guns protect your retreat. Understanding the role of each weapon position and when to use which ammunition type is the difference between sinking in 30 seconds and capturing a galleon intact.

⚔️ 2. Land Combat — Parries, Dodges & Weapon Combos

Land combat fundamentals are straightforward but have surprising depth. Light attacks (LMB) deal 100% base damage, cost 8 stamina, and chain up to 4 hits. Heavy attacks (RMB) deal 200% base damage, cost 20 stamina, and can break enemy posture. The parry window is 0.35 seconds — tap block (hold RMB then release) at the moment of enemy impact. A successful parry staggers the enemy for 2 seconds, during which all damage is increased by 50%. Failed parries drain 30 stamina and leave you vulnerable for 1 second.

The Cutlass Combo Every Player Should Know

LMB-LMB-RMB-LMB — Two fast slashes into a heavy overhead chop, then a quick thrust finisher. This combo deals approximately 550% total weapon damage and can be completed in 2.5 seconds. The heavy overhead (third hit) has a high stagger chance against human enemies. The thrust finisher (fourth hit) tracks moving targets, making it reliable against enemies who try to dodge. This is the bread-and-butter combo for cutlass users from the first hour to the final boss.

⚔️ 3. Naval Combat Basics — Cannons, Positioning & Wind

Naval combat is where Windrose truly distinguishes itself from other survival games. Every ship carries cannons on multiple firing arcs: broadside (left and right, most cannons, highest damage output), bow chasers (front, 1-2 cannons, used while pursuing), and stern guns (rear, 1-2 cannons, used while retreating). A Brigantine carries 12 cannons per broadside — unleashing a full broadside at close range can devastate an enemy ship in a single volley.

The single most important naval tactic is crossing the T — positioning your ship perpendicular to the enemy's bow or stern so that your full broadside faces their narrowest profile. This maximizes your firepower while minimizing theirs. Achieving this requires superior speed and wind management. Always approach enemy ships from upwind — you control the engagement. Ships approaching from downwind are slow, predictable, and easy to cross.

⛵ 4. Boarding Actions — When & How

Boarding is high-risk, high-reward. When you grapple an enemy ship (requires Grappling Hooks item, crafted at Workbench), your crew (or you, in solo) swings aboard and initiates close-quarters combat. The reward: you capture the ship intact, gaining 100% of its cargo, cannons, and the ship itself (which can be repaired and added to your fleet or salvaged for rare materials). Sinking a ship with cannons destroys 70-80% of its loot. Boarding preserves everything.

When to board: the enemy crew is below 50% (fewer defenders), the enemy ship is disabled (mast destroyed, unable to flee), and you have a clear numerical advantage (boarding with 1 HP is suicide). When NOT to board: the enemy ship is a Galleon (massive crew, you will be overwhelmed), the enemy ship is on fire (you will burn with it), or you are fighting multiple ships (you will be shot to pieces while immobilized during boarding). Boarding is a calculated risk, not a default strategy.

⛵ 6. Cannon Types & Ammo

Round Shot (standard): Deals 100% damage to hull. Default ammunition. Use for general combat.
Chain Shot: Deals 40% hull damage but 200% damage to masts and sails. Use to disable enemy mobility. A ship with broken masts cannot flee and cannot maneuver — it is a sitting duck.
Grape Shot: Deals 150% damage to crew. Use before boarding to thin enemy numbers. A single broadside of grape shot can kill 4-6 crew members on a Brigantine.
Heated Shot: Deals 80% hull damage and sets the enemy ship on fire. Fire deals 5% of max hull durability per second. Use against heavily armored Galleons where raw damage is insufficient. Risk: fire can spread to your ship if you get too close.

❓ FAQ

Q: Can I fight sea monsters with a Raft?

No. Sea monsters require at least a Sloop with cannons. Attempting to fight them with a Raft (zero cannons) is impossible — you cannot damage them. Flee any sea monster encounter until you have cannons.

Q: Which weapon is best for beginners?

The Cutlass. It has the most forgiving parry window (0.35s), a balanced moveset, and works in all situations. The Boarding Axe hits harder but has tighter parry timing. The Harpoon is powerful but too slow for beginners who are still learning enemy patterns.

Q: Should I always try to board enemy ships?

No. Only board when: the enemy is alone (no supporting ships), the enemy crew is below 50%, and the ship is disabled (broken mast). Boarding a healthy Galleon with a full crew is suicide. Sometimes sinking a ship with cannons and collecting partial loot is the smarter play.
