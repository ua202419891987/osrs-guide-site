🚢 1. How to Get Your First Ship — Quest Chain Recap

Your first ship in Windrose is not purchased — it is earned through a quest chain that serves as the game's extended tutorial. This quest chain teaches you every core mechanic (mining, smelting, crafting, combat) and culminates in an NPC rewarding you with a free Raft. If you have already completed this quest chain, you can skip this section. If you are stuck on any step, this recap will get you back on track.

1

Stone Pickaxe → Copper Ore

Craft a Stone Pickaxe (2 wood + 3 stone at the Workbench). Use it to mine copper ore from the northern cave of your starting island. You need at least 6 copper ore to complete the full chain. Expect to fight 2-3 bats guarding the cave entrance — bring a Wooden Spear (3 wood + 2 stone + 1 rope).

2

Clay → Charcoal Kiln → Charcoal

Mine clay from riverbanks (use a shovel — craft one at the Workbench with 2 wood). Build a Charcoal Kiln (10 clay + 8 stone at Workbench). Place the Kiln, insert 5 wood, and wait 30 seconds. The Kiln produces 1 Charcoal per 5 wood. You need 4 charcoal to fuel your first Smelter run.

3

Smelter → Copper Ingots

Build a Smelter (15 clay + 10 stone + 5 wood). Place it near your Charcoal Kiln (they work together). Insert 2 Copper Ore + 1 Charcoal. Wait 20 seconds. Each smelt produces 1 Copper Ingot. You need 5 ingots for a Copper Pickaxe, plus additional ingots for the next steps.

4

Copper Pickaxe → Weaponsmithing Station

Craft a Copper Pickaxe (5 copper ingots at Workbench). Now mine more copper efficiently. Accumulate 15 copper ingots and 10 wood to build the Weaponsmithing Station. This station unlocks all melee weapon recipes and is the quest's critical trigger — once built, an NPC (the Shipwright) will appear at your base with a quest marker.

5

Copper Sword → Defeat the Cave Troll → Free Raft

Craft a Copper Sword (8 copper ingots at Weaponsmithing Station). The Shipwright NPC gives you a quest to defeat a Cave Troll in a nearby cave. The Troll has approximately 150 HP and deals 35-50 damage per hit. Use the parry strategy (described in our Beginner Guide) — parry its slow overhead slam, then land 2-3 hits during its recovery. Upon victory, return to the Shipwright. He rewards you with a free Raft blueprint and the materials to build it (20 wood, 10 cloth, 5 rope — he supplies these). Your first ship is now in your inventory, ready to be placed in the water.

Total Time Investment: A new player starting from nothing can complete this entire quest chain in 3-5 hours. An experienced player can do it in 90 minutes. If you are taking longer, you are likely missing a key resource (usually clay — see our Resource Map Guide for clay locations).

✅ 2. Pre-Departure Checklist — 10 Things You Must Check

This is the most important section in this entire guide. More Windrose players lose their inventory, their ship, and their progress to careless pre-departure mistakes than to any enemy in the game. Read this checklist. Print it. Tattoo it on your forearm. Okay, maybe not that last one — but take it seriously.

⛵ Pre-Departure Checklist — Tick Each Box Before You Sail

Checklist Pro Tip: Make this checklist a physical habit. Every time you prepare to sail, run through these 10 points out loud or in your head. After 10 times, it becomes muscle memory. The best Windrose players have a pre-departure routine as rigid as a real-world pilot's pre-flight checklist — because the consequences of forgetting something are just as real in-game.

🌬️ 3. Sailing Basics — Wind, Speed & Handling

Sailing in Windrose feels intuitive within 5 minutes but has surprising strategic depth that takes 10-15 hours to fully master. This section covers the mechanics you need for your first voyage and the advanced techniques you will need once you upgrade to a Sloop or Brigantine.

💨 Understanding the Wind System

Wind direction is displayed as a small arrow icon in the top-right corner of your HUD. The wind changes direction every 10-15 in-game minutes (approximately every 2-3 real-time minutes). Your ship's speed is directly tied to your angle relative to the wind:

Sailing AngleSpeed MultiplierWhen to Use
Downwind (wind from behind)1.5x base speedLong-distance travel when destination is downwind
Crosswind (wind from side)1.0x base speed (normal)Standard sailing, most common angle
Upwind (wind from front)0.6x base speedAvoid if possible; use zigzag if necessary
In Irons (directly into wind)0.2x base speed (barely moving)Never do this intentionally

🚤 Speed Management — Load Matters

Your ship's speed is also affected by its cargo load. A Raft with an empty inventory sails at 100% of its base speed. A Raft carrying a full inventory (400 weight) sails at approximately 70% of its base speed. This matters for two reasons: (1) an overladen ship is slower to escape threats, and (2) a slower ship spends more time at sea, which means more exposure to random encounters and more food/water consumption.

The 70% Rule: Never depart on a long voyage with more than 70% of your ship's cargo capacity filled. The speed penalty is not worth the extra resources you are carrying. Make two trips instead of one overladen trip.

🎯 Handling — Turning Radius & Docking

The Raft has the smallest turning radius of any ship in Windrose — this is its one major advantage. It can turn almost in place. Use this to your advantage when docking: approach the beach at a 45-degree angle, then turn hard into the wind (A or D key) to kill your speed before landing. A Raft beached on sand can be pushed back into deep water by standing behind it and holding E (interact key).

Docking Pro Tip: Always dock your ship on a sandy beach, never on rocks or a coral reef. Sandy beaches do zero damage to your hull when docking. Rocks and reefs deal 5-10 hull damage per contact. Over multiple voyages, this adds up to an unplanned sinking.

🗺️ Coastal Sailing Strategy (Recommended for First Voyages)

Until you have a Sloop with at least 1 cannon, always sail within sight of land. Coastal sailing has three advantages: (1) if your ship sinks, you can swim to shore and resurrect at your last bedroll, (2) you can duck into a friendly beach to repair or heal if attacked, and (3) you can use visual landmarks to navigate (follow the coastline). The open ocean is for experienced players with combat-capable ships. Your first 5-10 voyages should all be coastal.

🍖 4. Sea Survival — Food, Water & Repair Management

The ocean in Windrose is not just a highway between islands — it is an environment with its own survival rules. Food and water deplete faster at sea. Your ship's hull takes damage from multiple sources. And unlike on land, you cannot simply "pause" the game to deal with a problem. Sea survival is about anticipation and preparation.

🍗 Food & Water Consumption Rates

Food drain at sea: 1.5x the rate on land. A Coconut that lasts 8 minutes on land lasts 5 minutes and 20 seconds at sea. Water drain at sea: Also 1.5x. Plan your food and water accordingly — a 20-minute voyage requires 1.5x the sustenance of a 20-minute land expedition.

On-board cooking: You can cook on your ship if you place a Campfire on the deck. However, this is not recommended for a Raft (the Campfire takes up precious deck space and is a fire hazard). On larger ships (Sloop and above), an on-board Cooking Station is standard equipment. For Raft voyages, cook all your food before departing and bring only cooked meals.

🔧 Ship Repair — Hull Integrity Management

Your ship's hull has 100 hit points (for a Raft). Damage sources include: rock/reef collision (5-15 HP), enemy cannon fire (20-30 HP per hit), ramming by enemy ships (10-20 HP), and sea monster attacks (30-50 HP). When hull HP reaches zero, the ship sinks. All items in the ship's storage are lost. Your character is thrown into the water — if you are in deep ocean, a shark will spawn within 20 seconds.

Repair Method: Equip a Repair Hammer (craft: 3 wood + 2 stone). Swim to your ship (or stand on deck if you are already on board). Hold right-click and aim at the ship's hull. Each hammer strike restores 25 HP and consumes 10 hammer durability. A fully repaired ship and a freshly crafted hammer mean you can do 4 full repairs (100 HP each) before the hammer breaks.

Emergency Repair: If your ship is at 20% HP and you do not have a Repair Hammer, you can use Wood directly (5 wood = 10 HP repair). This is wildly inefficient but can save your life in an emergency. Always carry extra wood.

Sinking Survival Protocol: If your ship is sinking and you cannot repair it in time, throw your most valuable items into the water before the ship goes down. Items in the water float for 3-5 minutes before despawning. If you survive the sinking (swim to shore, respawn at bedroll), you can swim back to the sinking location and retrieve your floating items. Items in your inventory when you die from sinking are permanent lost — but items thrown into the water before death are not. This is the most important survival trick in Windrose naval gameplay.

⚔️ 5. Naval Combat — From Random Encounters to Victory

Your first voyage is unlikely to involve naval combat — a Raft has no weapons and should run from any threat. But once you have a Sloop (your first combat-capable ship), naval combat becomes a core part of the Windrose experience. This section covers the essentials.

🐙 Sea Monster Encounters

Sea monsters spawn randomly in deep water (more than 100 meters from any island). The most common first encounter is the Reef Serpent — a medium-sized sea creature that attacks by ramming your ship and then diving underwater to reposition. Here is how to handle a sea monster encounter:

  1. Assess your ship's HP. If you have less than 50% hull integrity, flee. Do not fight. Sail directly downwind and the monster (which is faster than a Raft but slower than a Sloop with wind) may lose interest after 1-2 minutes.
  2. If you must fight: Equip your ranged weapon (bow or pistol). Sea monsters have a "surface" phase (30 seconds) where they are vulnerable to player attacks, followed by a "dive" phase (15 seconds) where they are invulnerable and repositioning.
  3. During surface phase: Shoot the monster's head (deals 2x damage vs. body shots). A bow with iron arrows deals 25-35 damage per hit to a Reef Serpent. It has approximately 200 HP — 6-8 well-placed shots kills it.
  4. During dive phase: Repair your ship. The monster always surfaces near your ship — be ready with your weapon drawn when it reappears.
  5. Loot: Dead sea monsters drop Monster Meat (restores 50 hunger, but has a 30% chance of food poisoning — cook it first) and Serpent Scale (used in late-game armor crafting).

🔫 Enemy Ship Combat (Sloop and Above)

Once you have a Sloop with at least 1 cannon, you can engage enemy ships (pirate Sloops that patrol certain sea lanes). Naval combat in Windrose is turn-based-ish: you maneuver to get a cannon shot angle, fire, then reposition while your cannon reloads (15-second reload time).

⚓ Naval Combat Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Range Assessment: Cannons have a maximum range of approximately 80 meters. If the enemy is beyond this range, sail closer. If the enemy is inside 30 meters, they can attempt to board your ship (triggers close-quarters combat on your deck).

Step2 — Positioning: Maneuver so the enemy ship is broadside to your cannons (side-facing). A Sloop has 1 cannon per side — you need to present your side to the enemy to fire.

Step 3 — The Cannon Shot: Interact with the cannon (E key). Aim using mouse movement. Left-click to fire. Cannonballs deal 20-40 hull damage (depending on distance — closer = more damage).

Step 4 — Reposition: After firing, turn away or change distance. While your cannon reloads, you are vulnerable. Use the 15-second reload window to repair hull damage or reposition for a better angle.

Step 5 — Boarding (Optional): If you reduce the enemy ship's hull to 30% or lower, you can board it. Sail close (within 5 meters), throw a Grappling Hook (crafted from 3 rope + 2 iron), and your character will leap onto the enemy deck. Kill the enemy crew (2-4 pirates) and the ship is yours to loot or capture.

Naval combat is one of the fastest ways to accumulate rare resources — but only if you survive the fight. Do not attack enemy ships until you have at least iron-tier weapons and a ship with 2+ cannons.

🏝️ 6. Second Island Landing — Exploration & Temporary Camp

Your first landing on a new island — your "second island" (the first island being your starting island) — is a moment of both excitement and danger. New islands have unfamiliar terrain, uncharted enemy spawn points, and resources you may never have seen before. This section covers the proper way to land, explore, and establish a temporary presence on a new island.

⚓ Choosing a Landing Site

Not all beaches are created equal. When selecting a landing site on a new island, evaluate these four factors:

  1. Beach width: Wide sandy beaches are safest. Narrow beaches backed by cliffs mean you have nowhere to retreat if attacked.
  2. Nearby water: You will need fresh water. A beach within 50 meters of a river or spring is ideal. If there is no fresh water on the island, you need to bring extra water from your ship.
  3. Enemy proximity: Look at the tree line behind the beach. If you see bone piles, gravestones, or ruined structures, enemies spawn nearby. Choose a beach at least 100 meters away from any spawn indicator.
  4. Ship docking safety: The beach should have a gentle slope into the water (no sudden drop-offs). A gradual slope lets you easily push your ship back into deep water when it is time to leave.

🏕️ Establishing a Temporary Forward Camp

Before you explore deep into a new island, establish a temporary forward camp at your landing beach. This camp serves as your respawn point, your emergency shelter, and your resource staging area. A basic forward camp takes 10 minutes to build and costs very little:

1

Campfire (5 wood + 3 stone)

Place it on the sand above the high-tide line. This is your spawn point — if you die on this island, you respawn here. It also lets you cook food and purify water.

2

Bedroll (5 cloth + 3 wood)

Place it next to the Campfire. Sleeping skips the night (useful if you land at dusk) and restores 50% HP.

3

Storage Chest (10 wood)

Place it near the Campfire. As you explore and gather resources, offload excess weight into this chest. This keeps your inventory light for exploration and ensures you do not lose everything if you die while exploring.

4

Rainwater Collector (optional, 5 wood + 3 clay)

If the island has no fresh water source, build a Rainwater Collector. It slowly fills with drinkable water during rain. Not essential for short visits but critical for multi-day expeditions.

🗺️ Exploration Strategy — The expanding Circle Method

Once your forward camp is established, explore the island using the expanding circle method: start at your camp, walk 50 meters in one direction, fully explore that 50-meter radius, then return to camp. Next trip: 100 meters. Next: 150 meters. This ensures you always have a known safe path back to your camp and prevents you from getting lost in unfamiliar terrain.

New Island Resource Differences: Each island type has unique resources. Tropical islands (your starting biome) have abundant wood and fiber but lack metal ores. Foothills islands have iron but lack cloth (no fiber plants). Volcanic islands have sulfur but are extremely dangerous. Swamp islands have rare alchemical herbs but have difficult terrain (deep mud slows movement by 30%). Plan your exploration based on what resources you need.

🔔 7. Fast Travel Bell — Crafting & Usage

The Fast Travel Bell is one of the most game-changing items in Windrose. It allows you to instantly teleport between any two bells you have placed, eliminating the need to sail back and forth between your base and distant resource nodes or forward camps. If you have ever groaned at the idea of making yet another 10-minute sail to your Foothills iron mine, the Fast Travel Bell is your solution.

🛠️ Crafting the Fast Travel Bell

Recipe: 10 Copper Ingots + 5 Iron Ingots + 3 Gold (if available) + 10 Wood. The exact recipe may vary based on EA version — check your in-game crafting menu. The Bell is crafted at the Advanced Smithing Station (unlocked after building an Anvil). This means you need to be well into the mid-game (copper + iron + some gold) before you can craft your first Bell.

Optimal Bell Placement Strategy: Place Bell #1 at your main base (near your Storage Chests). Place Bell #2 at your primary Foothills iron mining camp. Place Bell #3 at your Volcanic sulfur mining camp. This gives you instant access to the three most distance-critical resource nodes. Use Bell #4 and #5 for forward camps on expedition islands. When you are done with an expedition, pick up the Bell (it can be dismantled and moved) and relocate it to your next destination.

⚖️ Fast Travel vs. Normal Sailing — When to Use Each

Use Fast Travel Bell when: You need to shuttle resources between distant locations. You died and need to retrieve your items from a distant island. You need to check on a distant base or forward camp. You are low on food/water and need to return to base immediately.

Use Normal Sailing when: You need to transport your ship (Bells do not teleport ships). You are exploring and do not yet have a Bell placed at your destination. You are playing co-op and need to travel with your teammates (Bell teleports only you, not your team). You enjoy the sailing gameplay (some players find sailing relaxing and prefer it over teleportation).

👥 8. Crew Hiring & Co-Op Sailing

Windrose supports both solo and co-op play. In solo mode, you can hire NPC crew members to man your ship's cannons, repair hull damage during combat, and fight alongside you when you board enemy ships. In co-op mode (up to 4 players), your friends fill these roles manually. This section covers both systems.

🤝 NPC Crew Hiring (Solo Players)

NPC crew can be hired at Tavern structures. Taverns are found in certain NPC settlements (marked with a tankard icon on your map). Hiring one crew member costs 50 Gold (a mid-game currency — do not worry about this in your first 15 hours). Crew members have three stats: Cannon Accuracy (affects hit rate), Repair Speed (affects hull repair rate), and Melee Combat (affects damage in boarding actions).

Crew Management: Assigned crew members occupy seats on your ship. A Raft has 0 crew seats (you are alone). A Sloop has 1 crew seat. A Brigantine has 2 crew seats. A Galleon has 4 crew seats. Choose your crew members' stat allocation based on your ship size and your playstyle. For a Sloop (1 crew seat), prioritize Cannon Accuracy — one well-placed cannon shot is worth more than a crew member who fights adequately in boarding actions you may never attempt.

🎮 Co-Op Sailing (Multiplayer)

Windrose co-op supports up to 4 players on the same server. When sailing together, one player is the Captain (controls the ship's rudder and sails) and the other players are Crew (man cannons, repair, scout from the crow's nest). Coordination is everything — a Captain who tries to steer while also manning a cannon is half as effective at both tasks.

Recommended Co-Op Roles: Player 1 = Captain (steering, sail management, high-level tactical calls). Player 2 = Cannon Specialist (manages all cannon shots, calls out enemy positions). Player 3 = Repair & Support (monitors hull HP, repairs during combat, manages food/water for the team). Player 4 = Boarding Specialist (leads boarding actions, melee combat on enemy decks).

Co-Op Pro Tip: Use voice chat or the in-game text chat to coordinate. A co-op team that communicates is 3x more effective than a team that silently does their own thing. The single most common co-op mistake is the Captain sailing into combat while the rest of the team is still on shore gathering resources. Always confirm everyone is on board before departing.

💀 9. Top 5 Fatal Sailing Mistakes

Every Windrose player makes these mistakes. The difference between a beginner and an experienced player is that the experienced player made them already and learned. Read this list so you can skip straight to the "experienced" part.

#1 Most Fatal Mistake: Sailing Without Repairing the Ship

This is, without question, the #1 cause of shipwrecks in Windrose. A player gets excited about exploring a new island, hops on their Raft with 55% hull integrity, hits a rock 3 minutes into the voyage, and watches their ship sink into the abyss. The Rule: Never sail with less than 80% hull integrity. Repair before you depart. Always. No exceptions. Not even for "just a quick trip to the nearby island."
#2 Most Fatal Mistake: Insufficient Food Supply

Food drains 1.5x faster at sea. A voyage you expect to take 15 minutes (based on your land exploration experience) actually takes 20-25 minutes because of wind conditions, and you run out of food 10 minutes in. The Rule: Pack 2x the food you think you need. If you think 5 coconuts is enough, pack 10. Running out of food at sea is not immediately lethal (you do not die — your HP just starts draining slowly), but it puts you in a dangerously weakened state if you are then attacked by enemies upon landing.
#3 Most Fatal Mistake: Sailing Too Far from Coast Into High-Danger Zones

The deep ocean (more than 150 meters from any island) is where the most dangerous random encounters spawn: high-level sea monsters, pirate patrols, and rogue waves (in certain weather conditions). Early-game players should never be in deep water. The Rule: Stay within sight of land until you have a combat-capable ship (Sloop with at least 1 cannon). The coastline is your safety net. Use it.
#4 Most Fatal Mistake: Not Checking Wind Direction Before Setting Course

You plot a course to a distant island, sail for 10 minutes, and realize you have been fighting a headwind the entire time — your effective speed is 60% of normal and what should have been a 15-minute trip is now 25 minutes. Meanwhile, your food is running low and a sea monster has spawned because you have been sailing in the same area for too long. The Rule: Check wind direction before every voyage. Sail with the wind whenever possible. If your destination is upwind, either wait for a wind shift or plot a zigzag course.
#5 Most Fatal Mistake: Panicking During a Sea Monster Encounter

A new player sees the "Sea Monster Approaching" warning on their HUD, panics, turns the ship directly into the wind (the slowest possible configuration), and tries to outrun the monster by holding W (accelerate) while sailing at 60% speed. The monster catches up, rams the ship once, and the ship sinks because it was already at 40% hull from rock collisions. The Rule: When threatened at sea, breathe. Assess. Act calmly. A calm player can outmaneuver almost any threat. A panicking player sinks their own ship.

❓ FAQ — First Voyage Questions

Q: Can I get my first ship without completing the entire quest chain?

No. The free Raft is the reward for completing the full quest chain (Stone Pickaxe → Copper → Clay → Charcoal → Smelter → Copper Ingots → Copper Pickaxe → Weaponsmithing Station → Copper Sword → Defeat Cave Troll). There is no shortcut. However, once you have the Raft blueprint, you can craft additional Rafts at any time (20 wood + 10 cloth + 5 rope each).

Q: What do I do if my ship sinks in the middle of the ocean?

If your ship sinks, you are thrown into the water. Swim to the nearest island (look for the nearest land marker on your compass). Once on land, you will either respawn at your last bedroll/campfire (if you placed one on that island) or you will need to walk/swim to your main base. Losing a ship is frustrating but not game-ending — you can always craft a new Raft. The bigger loss is usually the inventory that went down with the ship.

Q: How do I defeat a sea monster if my Raft has no weapons?

You cannot defeat a sea monster with a Raft. Your only option is to flee. Sail downwind (fastest escape). If the monster catches you anyway, swim away from your ship (the monster may target the ship, giving you time to reach shore). Once you have a Sloop with a cannon, you can fight sea monsters. Until then: run, do not fight.

Q: Can I fast travel with my ship using the Fast Travel Bell?

No. The Fast Travel Bell teleports you only — not your ship. Your ship remains at the Bell location. If you teleport to your base while your ship is at a distant island, you will need to sail a new ship (or walk/swimif you are close enough) to retrieve your original ship. This is the main limitation of the Bell system and prevents it from making sailing completely irrelevant.

Q: How much does it cost to hire an NPC crew member?

Based on current EA information, hiring an NPC crew member costs 50 Gold at a Tavern. Gold is a mid-game currency obtained from looting chests in advanced ruins, defeating pirate ships, and selling rare resources to certain NPCs. You will not have Gold in your first 10-15 hours of play, which is intentional — the game expects you to sail solo until you are established enough to afford crew.

Q: What is the maximum number of players in co-op?

Windrose supports up to 4 players in a co-op session. One player hosts the server (their world is the "main" world), and up to 3 other players can join. All players share the same map, the same quest progress (triggered by the host), and can collaborate on building, sailing, and combat. If the host leaves, the session ends for everyone — there is no dedicated server mode in the current EA build.

Q: My ship is stuck on a reef and I cannot get it unstuck. What do I do?

This is a common problem, especially with larger ships (Brigantine and Galleon) that have deeper drafts. Solution 1: Get off the ship, stand behind it in the water, and hold E (interact) to push it. Solution 2: If pushing does not work, dismantle and rebuild the ship (you get 50% of materials back). Prevention: Always dock on sandy beaches with gradual slopes. Avoid coral reefs and rocky coastlines.

Q: How do I know which islands are safe for a first-time landing?

Tropical islands (green on the map) are always safe for first landings — they have passive wildlife and no aggressive enemy spawns near the beaches. Rocky islands (grey) may have skeleton pirates near ruins. Swamp islands (dark green) have venomous snakes. Volcanic islands (red) are never safe for first landings — they require iron armor and fire resistance. When in doubt, land on a Tropical island first and use it as a forward camp to scout other nearby islands.
