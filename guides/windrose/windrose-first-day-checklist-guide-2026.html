Minute 0-5: Washing Ashore â?Immediate Survival Priorities

Your journey begins on a small, procedurally generated starter island. You have nothing except torn clothes and empty hands. A tutorial message may flash briefly, but it disappears quickly and covers only the bare minimum. Do not rely on it. Follow this sequence from the moment you regain control of your character.

1

Gather Wood (First 60 Seconds)

Look around for the nearest trees. Punch them (left-click repeatedly) until they fall. You need 10 logs minimum. Do not stop at 5. The first 10 logs are your seed capital â?they become your axe, your campfire, and your emergency shelter. Punching trees takes roughly 4 hits per log. Clear a small patch of 3-4 trees and collect every log. Do not bother collecting branches or twigs from the ground â?they yield only 1 wood per pickup versus 3 from a full tree. The time-to-resource ratio favors trees 3:1.

2

Gather Stone (Seconds 60-120)

Walk along the shoreline. Stone nodes appear as grey, rounded rocks embedded in the sand and grass. You need 8 stones minimum â?3 for the axe, 3 for the pickaxe, and 2 to spare. Stone is less common than wood on starter islands. If you struggle to find 8 stones, look near cliff bases and cave entrances. Stone respawns every 5 minutes, so mark the locations of exhausted nodes mentally. You will return later.

3

Collect Fiber (Seconds 120-180)

Fiber plants look like tall grass with white tufts at the top. They grow near water sources â?rivers, ponds, and the edges of freshwater pools. You need 10 fiber minimum. Fiber is used for cloth (essential for the bedroll, raft, and armor). Each plant gives 2-3 fiber. Collect from 4-5 plants. You will need significantly more fiber later, but for the first 5 minutes, 10 pieces is your target.

4

Find Freshwater (Seconds 180-300)

Follow the coastline or look for darker green vegetation clusters â?these indicate freshwater. Stand in the water and press E to drink. Drink until the thirst icon (droplet) is full. Freshwater is essential within the first 5 minutes because your starting thirst bar is already at 50% â?it drains during the shipwreck cutscene. Do not prioritize food yet. Hunger drains slower than thirst during the first 15 minutes. Water is the more urgent need at this stage.

First 5 Minutes Checklist: 10 wood, 8 stone, 10 fiber, full thirst bar. If you have all four, you have successfully completed the most critical window of your entire playthrough. You now have the raw resources to survive the next hour. Do not skip any of these four steps â?missing even one can cascade into a death spiral within 30 minutes.

Minute 5-30: First Tools & Campfire â?Your Crafting Foundation

With your raw materials secured, it is time to craft. Open the crafting menu (Tab key) and navigate to the Tools tab. Craft in this exact order â?the sequence matters because each tool unlocks the next step in the efficiency chain.

1

Stone Axe (Minute 5-7)

Cost: 2 wood + 3 stone. The Stone Axe is the most important first craft. It increases wood gathering speed by 5x â?from 4 punches per tree to a single swing. With the axe equipped, chop 20 additional logs in 2 minutes. Do not stop at 20 â?keep chopping until you have 30-40 logs stockpiled. Wood is the foundation of every structure and tool in Windrose. You cannot have too much wood on day one.

2

Stone Pickaxe (Minute 7-9)

Cost: 2 wood + 3 stone. The Pickaxe unlocks stone node mining. Stone nodes that gave you 1 stone per punch now give 3 stone per swing. Mine until you have 20 stone. Stone is used for the campfire, the furnace, defensive walls, and tool upgrades. Most beginners underestimate stone requirements and have to farm it later when they could have stockpiled it during the first 10 minutes.

3

Campfire (Minute 9-12)

Cost: 5 wood + 3 stone. Place your campfire in a central, defensible location â?not too close to the water (tide mechanics can extinguish it) and not too deep in the forest (enemies can approach unseen). The campfire serves three critical functions: it saves your spawn point (right-click â?"Set Respawn"), it cooks food (eliminating disease risk from raw meat), and it provides warmth against night temperature drops. Without a campfire, your first night is a death sentence.

4

Torch (Minute 12-15)

Cost: 1 wood + 2 fiber. Craft a Torch immediately after the campfire. The torch prevents the "Night Terror" debuff (reduces visibility to near-zero) and, as covered in our Hidden Mechanics Guide, negates skeleton debuffs. Equip the torch in your off-hand slot. You will need it for the dusk transition around minute 20-25 of your playthrough, depending on when you started.

5

Hunt and Cook (Minute 15-30)

With tools and campfire ready, hunt 2-3 boars. Boars are found in forested areas and near berry bushes. Approach slowly â?boars charge when you get within 10 meters. Dodge the charge (Spacebar + sideways), then hit them 3-4 times with the Stone Axe (it doubles as a melee weapon). Each boar drops 2-4 Raw Meat and 1 Leather. Return to your campfire, open the cooking menu, and cook all meat. Cooked boar meat restores 30 hunger (versus 15 from raw meat) and has zero disease chance. Eat one piece and save the rest for exploration.

Tool Priority Diamond: The optimal early-game tool order is Stone Axe â?Stone Pickaxe â?Campfire â?Torch. Do not skip the Pickaxe for a weapon â?the Stone Axe is a perfectly adequate weapon for boars and skeletons on the starter island. Crafting the Pickaxe second instead of a weapon saves you roughly 10 minutes of stone farming time over the next hour.

Minute 30-60: Food, Water & First Night Survival

The transition from day to night in Windrose happens at approximately the 30-minute mark of your first day. You will know night is coming when the sky shifts from bright blue to orange, then purple, then near-black over the course of roughly 3 minutes. Use this twilight window to prepare.

1

Secure a Water Supply (Minute 30-35)

Find the nearest freshwater source and drink until full. Then craft a Waterskin (5 cloth + 2 rope). Rope is crafted from 3 cloth at your campfire. The Waterskin holds 3 servings of water and eliminates the need to return to the river every 5 minutes. Fill it immediately after crafting. A full Waterskin lasts roughly 15 minutes of active gameplay.

2

Berry Run (Minute 35-40)

While there is still light, harvest 15-20 berries from bushes. Berries are the fastest food source â?you can gather 10 in 30 seconds. They only restore 15% hunger each, but they are abundant and require no cooking. Store berries in your inventory as emergency backup food. Eat cooked meat first, and only dip into the berry reserve when meat runs out.

3

Build a Bedroll (Minute 40-45)

Cost: 5 cloth + 3 wood. The Bedroll lets you skip the night. Place it next to your campfire and right-click â?"Claim Bed." Sleeping until morning restores 50% health and advances time by roughly 8 in-game hours. Do not sleep immediately upon building the bed â?use the remaining daylight to gather more resources. Sleep only when the screen is nearly dark and you can no longer see resource nodes.

4

Night Gathering (Minute 45-60 â?Optional)

If you have a torch and are feeling confident, do not sleep through the first night. Night-time has hidden resource spawns (see our Hidden Mechanics Guide â?section 14). Check the beach and cave entrances for Glowing Mushrooms and Moonstone Ore. These resources are invisible during the day and only appear at night. Even if you do not need them immediately, stockpiling night resources gives you a massive head start on mid-game crafting. Use the torch in your off-hand and stay near your campfire. Do not venture into deep forests at night â?skeleton spawns triple after dark.

First Night Warning: Do not sleep outdoors without a campfire. Your character's temperature drops rapidly during Windrose nights. Hypothermia sets in after 30 seconds of cold exposure, draining health at 5 HP per second. A campfire within 5 meters prevents hypothermia. A torch alone is insufficient â?it only slows the temperature drop by 50%. Always have a campfire nearby before you sleep.

Hour 1-2: The Workbench & Intermediate Crafting

With the first night survived, your priority shifts from pure survival to production efficiency. The Workbench is the gateway to every mid-range item in Windrose. It unlocks the Raft blueprint, the Cooking Station, Storage Chests, and the Tanning Rack. Without a workbench, you are stuck with campfire-tier crafting forever.

1

Gather Additional Materials (Hour 1-1:15)

Before building the workbench, gather: 30 wood (total from earlier farming), 15 stone, and 10 fiber. The workbench itself costs 15 wood + 10 stone. You also want the resources for a Storage Chest (10 wood) and a Cooking Station (8 wood + 5 stone) â?build these immediately after the workbench. A single gathering run of 15 minutes should net everything you need.

2

Build the Workbench (Hour 1:15-1:20)

Place the workbench near your campfire, ideally within the Comfort radius of any makeshift shelter you have built (even a 3-wall lean-to provides minor comfort). Once built, interact with the workbench to unlock a massive new crafting catalog. Your immediate priorities: the Cooking Station (replaces campfire cooking with 4-slot capacity and zero disease chance), a Storage Chest (keeps resources safe and organized), and the Tanning Rack (converts boar leather into usable Leather rolls).

3

Craft Armor (Hour 1:20-1:40)

With the Tanning Rack operational, craft a full set of Leather Armor. Cost per piece: Chest â?5 Leather, Legs â?4 Leather, Boots â?2 Leather (3 Leather total). Leather comes from boar hides processed at the Tanning Rack (2 hides â?1 Leather). You need roughly 10 boar kills for a full armor set. Leather Armor provides 40% damage reduction against physical attacks â?the difference between dying in 3 skeleton hits and surviving 7. Do not explore new islands without at least the chest piece.

4

Upgrade Tools (Hour 1:40-2:00)

The workbench unlocks the upgrade path from stone to iron tools. You need iron ore â?found in rocky outcrop biomes (grey on the map). However, if your starter island is purely tropical (green), you may not have iron ore available. In that case, build at least 2 storage chests, fill them with your stockpiled wood and stone, and prepare to leave the island. The workbench is the signal that you are ready for the wider archipelago.

Efficiency Checkpoint: By the end of hour 2, you should have: a workbench, a cooking station, a storage chest, a tanning rack, full leather armor (at least chest + legs), a waterskin, a torch, and 40+ wood in reserve. If you have all of these, you are ahead of the average new player curve. If you are missing any of these, delay your departure and spend another 30 minutes gathering.

Hour 2-3: Build Your Raft â?Escape the Starter Island

The Raft is the single most important craft in the entire game. It is your ticket off the starter island and into the archipelago. Without it, you are trapped. With it, the entire game opens up. The Raft blueprint becomes available at the Workbench after you have built a campfire and cooked at least one piece of meat (this triggers the "Survivor" status, which unlocks the Raft recipe â?another hidden mechanic the game does not explain).

Raft Recipe: 20 wood, 10 cloth, 5 rope. Cloth is crafted from fiber at a ratio of 3 fiber â?1 cloth. Rope is crafted from 3 cloth. The math: you need 30 fiber for 10 cloth, plus 15 more fiber for the 5 rope (which costs 15 cloth, but cloth is already made from fiber). In total: 45 fiber, 20 wood. If you have been gathering fiber steadily since minute 0, you should have this by hour 2. If not, spend 20 minutes farming fiber plants near the coast.

Hidden Mechanic: Building the Raft in the correct location matters. If you build it on the beach at low tide, it may be stranded inland when the tide rises. Wait â?the tide? Yes, Windrose has a tide cycle. High tide submerges the lower 30% of the beach. Build your Raft at the treeline edge, not the waterline. Drag it into the water by interacting with the Raft and selecting "Push to Water." This takes 10 seconds and costs no stamina. Do not build the Raft in the water â?the crafting UI bugs out in knee-deep water and you may lose materials.

Before launching, equip your Raft with a sail (automatic with the craft) and a Repair Hammer (3 wood + 2 stone, crafted at workbench). The Repair Hammer lets you fix hull damage mid-voyage. Also place a Storage Chest on your Raft (cost: 10 additional wood) â?this gives you 30 inventory slots exclusively for ship cargo. Without a chest, your personal inventory fills up after 2 island stops.

Hour 3-4: Choosing Your First Real Island â?What to Look For

You have built your Raft. You have packed supplies. Now comes the most consequential decision of your first day: which island to sail to first. Your starter island is safe but resource-poor. The islands you can reach within a 5-minute sail from your starter island fall into three categories, ranked by priority.

Priority 1: Small Tropical Island with Visible Clay Deposits. Clay appears as reddish-brown patches on the ground near freshwater sources. Clay is the most important early-game resource that most guides overlook (detailed in section 7). A tropical island with clay is your ideal first destination. These islands are typically marked by a ring of white sand and clustered palm trees. Approach from the leeward side (the side opposite the wind direction) for an easier landing.

Priority 2: Rocky Outcrop Island with Iron Ore. These islands appear as grey-brown mounds with steep cliffs. They are harder to land on â?you need to find a sandy cove or a natural ramp â?but they contain iron ore nodes. Iron ore unlocks iron tools, which are 3x more durable than stone tools and mine 2x faster. A rocky outcrop that also has clay deposits is a jackpot â?settle here immediately.

Priority 3: Medium Tropical Island with a Freshwater Lake. If neither clay nor iron is available, look for a tropical island with a visible lake or river (easier to spot from the ship â?water reflects sunlight). These islands have abundant wood and fiber, plus passive wildlife for food. They are safe places to establish a forward base while you scout for better resource islands.

Island Selection Rule: Never settle on an island without freshwater. Islands without rivers, lakes, or ponds force you to collect rainwater (requires a Rain Catcher blueprint from the workbench, and rain is unreliable). An island with no freshwater will kill you within 15 minutes of arrival. Check for freshwater before you dock â?sail within 20 meters of the shore and look for dark green vegetation clusters, which only grow near water.

How to spot islands while sailing: Windrose renders islands as silhouettes on the horizon. Their color tells you the biome category. Green silhouettes = tropical (safe, abundant resources). Grey silhouettes = rocky (minerals, iron, but fewer food sources). Dark green = swamp (dangerous, but rich in rare alchemy ingredients). Red/brown = volcanic (endgame, avoid on day one). Sail toward green or grey silhouettes. Ignore swamp and volcanic until you have iron weapons.

Hour 4-6: Clay Is Critical â?Why You Need It Immediately

Clay is the most undervalued resource in Windrose beginner guides. Most guides mention wood, stone, iron, and fiber, then skip clay entirely. This is a mistake. Clay is the key to the Forge â?the crafting station that smelts iron ore into iron ingots and unlocks the entire metal tool tier. Without clay, you cannot build a Forge. Without a Forge, you are stuck with stone tools forever.

The Forge blueprint costs: 10 clay, 5 stone, 5 wood. Clay is also used in: the Furnace (advanced smelting, 5 clay), the Brick Wall (superior defensive wall, 10 clay per segment), the Cooking Pot (upgrades Cooking Station to tier 3 Kitchen, 8 clay), and the Water Well (infinite freshwater, 15 clay + 10 stone). Every single one of these structures is locked behind clay availability.

1

How to Find Clay (Hour 4-4:15)

Clay deposits appear as reddish-brown, cracked earth patches. They are always near freshwater â?rivers and lake edges are the most reliable locations. On a clay-bearing island, look for darker, red-tinted soil between the treeline and the water. Use your Stone Pickaxe to mine it. Each clay node yields 3-5 clay. You need roughly 25 clay for your first day's projects (Forge + Water Well). Mine every clay node on your chosen island â?they do not respawn quickly (roughly 30-minute cycle).

2

Build the Forge (Hour 4:15-4:30)

With 10 clay secured, build the Forge near your workbench. The Forge requires fuel (wood or coal) and ore. Smelt your iron ore into iron ingots at a 2:1 ratio (2 ore â?1 ingot). Each iron tool costs 3-5 ingots. Prioritize an Iron Pickaxe (3 ingots) â?it mines stone and clay 2x faster than stone and lasts 3x longer. Then craft an Iron Axe (3 ingots) for faster wood gathering. Iron weapons (Iron Sword, 4 ingots) are optional at this stage â?the Stone Axe remains viable for combat.

3

Build the Water Well (Hour 4:30-5:00)

If you settled on an island without a convenient river, the Water Well is your next priority. Cost: 15 clay + 10 stone. The Water Well provides unlimited freshwater from a single tile. Place it near your workbench area. Right-click â?"Drink" fills your entire thirst bar in 2 seconds. This eliminates the need to run to a river every 5 minutes and removes the dysentery risk of drinking from natural sources. The Water Well is a quality-of-life upgrade that pays for itself within 30 minutes.

Clay Farming Route: If your chosen island has limited clay, build a Raft with a Storage Chest and explore nearby islands. Load every clay node you find into the chest. A 1-hour clay-farming voyage across 3-4 islands can net 50-80 clay â?enough for all your first-week projects. Mark clay-rich islands on your map with a "Clay" Landmark Flag so you can return for the respawn cycle.

Hour 6-8: First Base & Faction Choice

By hour 6, you should have a workbench, a forge, a water well, iron tools, leather armor, and a raft with storage. You are now ready to build a permanent base and choose your faction allegiance. These two decisions are deeply connected â?your faction choice determines which exclusive blueprints you can access in the first week.

Base Location Criteria: The ideal first base island has all four of these: abundant wood (forest covers at least 30% of the island), iron ore nodes (at least 3 visible nodes), clay deposits (at least 2 patches near freshwater), and a natural harbor (a cove or inlet where your ship can dock safely). Tropical islands with rocky outcrop sections are the perfect hybrid. The island does not need to be large â?a small-to-medium island with all four resources is better than a large island missing any of them.

Defensive Base Layout: Build a wooden palisade wall around your core base area (workbench, forge, storage, bed). The wall should have a single entrance â?place a door here. Inside, arrange your crafting stations in a U-shape around your campfire or Cooking Station. Place storage chests along the inner walls. This layout minimizes running distance and creates natural chokepoints against any enemies that breach the wall. Leave space for future expansion â?you will add a Kitchen, an Alchemy Station, and crew quarters later.

Which Faction to Join First: The Iron Fleet. The three factions become available after you build your first base and craft a Ship Dock. A faction emissary will visit your dock within 1 in-game day of building it. Talk to them to start the faction alignment quest. Our recommendation: join The Iron Fleet first. Their early rewards include the Iron Saber (a tier 2 weapon that outclasses everything craftable at the workbench), the Hull Reinforcement blueprint (doubles your ship's durability), and the Combat Training dummy (boosts your parry timing window by 0.1 seconds). These rewards accelerate your progression more than The Golden Charter's trade bonuses or The Free Sailors' exploration perks in the first week.

Faction Lock-In Warning: Once you accept a faction quest, you are locked into that faction for 3 in-game days (roughly 90 minutes real time). You cannot switch during the cooldown. Choose carefully â?if you accept The Golden Charter quest but realize you need combat upgrades for an upcoming boss, you must wait 90 minutes before you can take an Iron Fleet quest. The three-day cooldown is a hidden mechanic that punishes impulsive faction choices.

Base Expansion Roadmap for Days 2-3: After establishing your base and joining The Iron Fleet, your next priorities are: upgrade your Raft to a Sloop (requires the cannon quest â?see Hidden Mechanics Guide section 4), build a Kitchen (improves food quality and buff duration), construct a second storage wing (you will need 3-4 chests by day 3), and craft a bed for NPC crew (once you start recruiting, they need somewhere to sleep). By the end of day 3, your base should have running water (well), unlimited food (kitchen + fishing rotation), and a combat-capable ship.

Avoiding the 5 Most Common First-Day Deaths

Based on community data from over 10,000 Windrose players, these five mistakes account for 78% of all first-day deaths. Memorize them and avoid them.

Death #1: Shark Attack While Swimming

Frequency: 31% of first-day deaths. The cause: swimming more than 30 meters from shore. The fix: never swim beyond where your feet can touch the bottom. If you need to cross water, build a raft. If your raft sinks, do not try to swim to the nearest island â?you will attract a shark within 15 seconds. Instead, swim back to shore and rebuild. Sharks deal 40 damage per bite and you have roughly 80 HP on day one. Two bites = death.

Death #2: Hypothermia at Night

Frequency: 22% of first-day deaths. The cause: sleeping outdoors without a campfire, or wandering too far from your heat source after dark. The fix: always place your campfire within 5 meters of your bedroll. If you need to explore at night, stay within torch range of the campfire and return to warm up every 2-3 minutes. The hypothermia warning â?screen edges turning blue â?gives you approximately 8 seconds before health drain begins. React immediately.

Death #3: Starvation/Dehydration Loop

Frequency: 15% of first-day deaths. The cause: letting both hunger and thirst drop to zero simultaneously. When both bars are empty, health drains at 8 HP per second â?twice the rate of either alone. The fix: always keep one bar above 30%. If you are out of food, drink water â?water alone keeps you alive longer than food alone. Berries grow everywhere and take 5 seconds to gather. Never let both bars hit zero.

Death #4: Skeleton Ambush in Caves

Frequency: 6% of first-day deaths. The cause: entering caves on the starter island without a torch or weapon. Caves spawn 2-4 skeleton pirates who emerge from the darkness and surround you. The fix: never enter a cave without a torch in your off-hand and a weapon drawn. The torch negates the Skeleton Fear debuff (see Hidden Mechanics, section 6). Fight skeletons one at a time by backing into a corner â?they cannot surround you there.

Death #5: Eating Raw Meat

Frequency: 4% of first-day deaths. The cause: eating raw meat when starving. Raw meat has a 40% chance of causing Food Poisoning â?a debuff that drains 3 HP per second for 20 seconds, deals nausea (blurred screen), and prevents eating or drinking for 10 seconds. Many players eat raw meat to stave off starvation, then die from the poison damage. The fix: never eat raw meat. Cook everything. If you have no campfire, eat berries instead. Berries have zero disease risk.

Death Prevention Mantra: "Campfire first, then cook, then explore." Repeat this to yourself every time you land on a new island. The first thing you do on a new shore is build a temporary campfire. It saves your spawn point, lets you cook, and prevents hypothermia. The 2 minutes it takes to build a campfire on arrival is the best insurance policy in Windrose.

When to Leave the Starter Island â?The Right Moment

This is the most common question new players ask, and the answer is counterintuitive: leave the starter island earlier than you think you should. Many beginners spend 8-10 hours on the starter island, building elaborate bases, stockpiling thousands of wood, and exploring every corner. This is a trap. The starter island is designed as a tutorial space with intentionally limited resources. It has no iron, no clay (in most seeds), no silver, and no faction emissaries. You cannot progress past stone tools on the starter island.

The optimal departure checklist: Leave when you have all of the following: A Raft with a Storage Chest, a Repair Hammer, 20 cooked meat in inventory, a full Waterskin, a Torch, Leather Armor (at least chest), 50+ wood in ship storage, and a destination picked (look for green/grey islands within 5 minutes sail). This typically happens around hour 2-3 of a focused playthrough. If you have all these items and it has been 4+ hours, you have been moving too slowly â?gather what you need and push off immediately.

What to leave behind: You do not need to dismantle your starter base. Leave the campfire, the workbench, and any spare materials. You may return to the starter island later for nostalgia or as a emergency respawn point. Do not waste time dismantling structures â?the resources you recover (50% of build cost) are not worth the 15 minutes of hammer time. Focus forward momentum. The starter island's only value is as your first step into a much larger world.

The Golden Rule of Windrose Progression: Your starter island is a bus stop, not a home. The moment you have a Raft and a destination, leave. The resources on the next island are 3x more valuable. The progression on the next island is 5x faster. Every minute you spend on the starter island after building your Raft is a minute you are not spending upgrading to iron, finding clay, joining a faction, and building the ship that will carry you to the endgame. Sail early, sail often.

FAQ â?First Day Questions

Q: What happens if I die on day one?

You respawn at your last claimed campfire or bedroll with your full inventory intact. You do not lose items on death in Windrose. However, if you die at sea, your Raft remains at the location where you died â?swim back to shore, build a second Raft, and sail to retrieve your original one. If the Raft sinks before you return, its storage chest contents are lost forever.

Q: Can I avoid sleeping on the first night?

Yes. If you have a torch and a campfire, you can survive the night without sleeping. Night-time has exclusive resource spawns (Moonstone Ore, Glowing Mushrooms) that are worth collecting. However, staying awake through the first night is risky â?skeleton spawns triple, and visibility is severely limited. Only attempt this if you have leather armor and an iron weapon. For most players, sleeping through the first night is the safer choice.

Q: How do I get more inventory space?

Your personal inventory has 20 slots by default. You can expand it by crafting a Backpack (10 cloth + 5 rope at the workbench), which adds 10 slots. The Backpack is equipped in the armor/accessory slot â?it does not conflict with chest armor. Additionally, every ship you build can have a Storage Chest installed, giving you 30 more slots per chest. On day one, your Raft chest is your primary storage expansion.

Q: What is the best weapon for day one?

The Stone Axe. It is not technically a weapon â?its combat stats are identical to the Stone Hatchet â?but it is the tool you already have equipped. Crafting a dedicated Stone Spear (5 wood + 3 stone) is a waste of materials on day one. The Stone Axe deals 15 damage per hit (same as the Spear) but also gathers wood. By the time you need a real weapon (hour 4+), you should be crafting an Iron Saber through The Iron Fleet faction questline.

Q: How long does the first day last in real time?

One full Windrose day-night cycle lasts roughly 30 minutes real time. Daytime is approximately 20 minutes, and nighttime is approximately 10 minutes. However, sleeping through the night (using a bedroll) skips the night phase instantly, effectively making your first day as short or as long as you choose. A "first day" of gameplay is typically 6-8 hours real time â?enough to complete all 10 sections of this guide.

Q: Should I play with friends on my first day?

Co-op makes day one significantly easier â?resources can be split, combat can be shared, and tasks can be parallelized. However, playing solo on your first playthrough is recommended. Windrose's solo difficulty is well-balanced, and learning to survive alone teaches you the mechanics more thoroughly than being carried by a friend. Save co-op for your second playthrough or for tackling endgame bosses.

Q: What is the worst mistake I can make on day one?

Swimming into deep water. Shark attacks account for 31% of all first-day deaths and are almost always fatal. The second worst mistake is eating raw meat during an emergency â?the food poisoning debuff kills you faster than starvation would have. The third worst: building a base on the starter island and investing hours into it before realizing the island has no iron, no clay, and no faction access.

Q: When should I start following this guide's timeline if I join a pre-existing world?

If you join a co-op session where other players have already progressed, skip directly to Section 5 (Build Your Raft) and Section 7 (Clay Is Critical). Ask your teammates for leather armor, a basic weapon, and a bed. The early gathering steps (Sections 1-4) assume you are starting from zero. In a group, you can typically reach the iron tool stage within 30 minutes of joining, rather than the 4 hours it takes a solo player.
