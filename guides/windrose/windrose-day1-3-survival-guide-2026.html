🌊 1. Before You Wake Up — How to Choose the Right Difficulty (Changing It Later Is Impossible!)

Before your character opens their eyes on the shoreline, Windrose presents you with a difficulty selection screen. This is the single most important decision you will make in your entire playthrough — and once you confirm your choice, it is permanently locked. There is no in-game menu to adjust difficulty later. The only way to change it is to delete your save and start a brand-new game. Read this section carefully before you click "Start".

Windrose offers four difficulty presets plus a fully customizable option. Each one dramatically changes the survival pressure, enemy aggression, and resource scarcity you will face. Picking the wrong difficulty will not just make the game "harder" — it can make the early game actively miserable if you are still learning the mechanics.

Calm Waters (Recommended for 90% of New Players)

Calm Waters is not "easy mode" in the sense of being boring. Enemies still attack you, you still need to manage hunger and thirst, and you can still die. The difference is that you have more room for error. Enemies deal 30% less damage and have 30% less health, meaning you can survive 2-3 extra hits while you are still learning dodge timing. Resource nodes (trees, rocks, berry bushes) yield 50% more materials per harvest, so you will spend less time grinding for basic supplies. If you have never played a survival crafting game before, or if you find action combat stressful, start here. You can always increase the challenge in your next playthrough.

High Seas (The Default Experience)

High Seas uses the developer-intended 100% values across the board. This is the "standard" Windrose experience that most guides and community discussions assume you are playing on. Enemy damage and health are at their designed levels. Resource gathering rates are balanced around a typical play session. If you have played games like Valheim, Raft, or ARK before, this difficulty will feel familiar. Choose this if you want the intended challenge without unnecessary frustration.

Storm's Edge (For the Masochists)

Storm's Edge increases enemy damage and health to 150% of normal. This means early-game boars can two-shot you if you have no armor. Resource scarcity means you will need to travel farther to find enough wood and stone to build your first workbench. This difficulty is designed for players who want a hardcore survival experience and are comfortable with frequent death and restarting. Only choose this if you have already beaten the game on High Seas or have extensive soulslike experience.

Captain's Choice (Full Customization)

Captain's Choice unlocks independent sliders for enemy damage (50%–500%), enemy health (50%–500%), resource abundance (50%–200%), hunger rate, and more. You can create a custom experience that suits your exact preferences. Want high damage but abundant resources? You can do that. Want a nightmare where everything hurts but you can build quickly? Also possible. This mode is best left for second or third playthroughs once you know what each setting actually does to the gameplay loop.

CRITICAL WARNING: Difficulty cannot be changed after you start. The option is greyed out in the settings menu. If you realize you picked the wrong difficulty, your only option is to delete your save file and start over. There is no mod or console command to bypass this. Choose carefully before you confirm!

⏰ 2. Hour 0-1: Beach Survival First Hour (5-Step Action Checklist)

Your character wakes up face-down in the sand. The waves are crashing. You have nothing — no tools, no weapon, no food, no water. The next 60 minutes will be the most dangerous time in your entire Windrose playthrough. If you follow the exact sequence below, you will survive the first night with full health, a workbench, and basic tools. Deviating from this order is the #1 reason new players quit within their first two hours.

The key to the first hour is efficiency. Every minute you spend wandering aimlessly is a minute you will feel later when darkness falls and you have no campfire. Stick to this checklist and move with purpose.

1

0:00 — Scavenge the Immediate Beach (0-10 Minutes)

Do not run inland yet. Stay on the sand and gather every loose item you can see. Look for driftwood (medium-sized logs partially buried in sand) and smooth stones (grey rounded rocks glinting in the sun). You need 5 wood and 5 stones minimum before you move to step 2. Also check the tide line for seashells — these are not just decoration. Collect at least 6 seashells. They are your emergency backup weapon (yes, really — see Section 7) and can be crafted into primitive tools. Do not waste time fighting anything yet. If a boar charges you, run into the water (boars will not follow into waist-deep water).

2

0:10 — Find Coconut Trees and Harvest 3-5 Coconuts (10-20 Minutes)

Look along the beach for coconut palms — tall trees with large green or brown fruits at their base. Coconuts are the best early-game food because they restore both hunger AND thirst simultaneously. No other early food does this. To harvest: walk up to the coconut and press E (interact). Each coconut restores 25% hunger and 20% thirst. You want 3-5 coconuts in your inventory before you do anything else. This food buffer means you will not starve or dehydrate while you focus on building. Pro tip: green coconuts (still on the tree) have a 30% chance to give you a drinking coconut that restores 40% thirst with zero disease risk. Climb the tree (hold Space near the trunk) to access them.

3

0:20 — Hunt a Boar for Coarse Leather (20-30 Minutes)

Now that you have food and water covered, you need coarse leather. This is required for your first boots and basic armor. Boars are found in the grassy areas just behind the beach. Approach slowly — boars have a short aggro range but will charge if you get too close. The strategy: wait for the boar to lower its head and snort (this is the tell). Dodge to the side (Spacebar + direction) as it charges, then hit it 2-3 times with your fists or a driftwood club (craft: 1 wood + 1 vine). After it dies, loot the carcass for raw boar meat (food) and coarse leather (material). You need 2 coarse leather for survivor boots. If you miss the dodge and take damage, eat a coconut immediately — do not let your health drop below 50%.

4

0:30 — Gather Plant Fiber (Universal Crafting Material) (30-40 Minutes)

Plant fiber is the duct tape of Windrose. It is used in almost every early recipe: tools, cords, primitive armor, bandages, and torches. Look for tall grass-like plants near the treeline — they have a subtle glow when you face them. Harvest by pressing E. You want at least 15 plant fiber before you move on. While you are gathering fiber, also look for vines hanging from trees or growing on the ground. Vines are used to craft ropes, which are required for better tools and your first backpack upgrade. Gather 5 vines if you see them, but do not waste time hunting for them — fiber is the priority. By now you should also have encountered a flint node (grey rock with white speckles). Mine it with a stone (hold left-click) to get 2-4 flint shards. Flint is required for sharp tools.

5

0:45 — Craft Your First Stone Axe (40-50 Minutes)

You now have: wood (5+), stones (5+), plant fiber (15+), flint (2+). Open your inventory (Tab) and navigate to the crafting tab. Craft a Stone Axe (1 wood + 1 stone + 1 plant fiber). The stone axe is a game-changer. It lets you chop trees 5x faster than punching them and mines stone nodes efficiently. Equip the axe (click it in your inventory). Now chop down 2-3 trees to get 10-15 additional wood. Then mine 2-3 stone nodes to get 10-15 additional stone. By minute 50, your inventory should contain: 20+ wood, 15+ stone, 2 coarse leather, 3+ coconuts, 10+ plant fiber, and 2+ flint.

6

1:00 — Start Chopping Trees for Wood Reserve (50-60 Minutes)

With your stone axe equipped, spend the final 10 minutes of hour 1 building a wood stockpile. You need 30+ wood total before you can build a workbench and campfire. Chop trees systematically — aim for the base of the trunk and rotate around the tree to avoid the falling animation hitting you (it deals 10 damage). By the end of hour 1, you should be standing next to a pile of 30-40 wood, 15-20 stone, and all the materials collected above. If you have time, also hunt a second boar for extra leather and meat. Cook the meat on a makeshift fire (pile 3 wood + 1 flint, then ignite with a torch) to get cooked boar meat — this restores 40% hunger with zero disease risk and provides a small health regen buff for 3 minutes.

Pro Tip — The 5-Minute Rule: If at any point during the first hour you have NOT collected the target material within 5 minutes of looking, move to a new area of the beach/island. Do not waste 10 minutes hunting for one specific resource node. Windrose's procedural generation means resources are distributed differently every game, but there is ALWAYS enough within 100 meters of your spawn point. Keep moving and looting efficiently.

🔥 3. Hour 1-3: First Campfire & Workbench (Material List + Build Order)

Hour 1-3 is the "infrastructure phase" of your Windrose playthrough. You have basic tools. Now you need to establish your first crafting infrastructure so you can stop surviving and start thriving. The build order in this phase matters enormously. Building structures in the wrong order will waste precious materials and could leave you without a campfire when night falls.

Build Priority #1: Campfire (Do This FIRST)

The campfire is the most important structure in early Windrose. It serves four critical functions: (1) it sets your respawn point — if you die, you wake up next to your campfire; (2) it allows you to cook raw food, eliminating disease risk and doubling the hunger restoration; (3) it provides warmth at night, preventing your character from taking cold damage; and (4) it acts as a light source, reducing the spawn rate of nocturnal enemies nearby.

Campfire recipe: 5 wood + 3 stone. Place it on flat ground (not on a slope — the campfire will tilt and look broken). Once placed, interact with it (E) to open the cooking menu. Place raw boar meat in the cooking slot. After 15 seconds, the meat is cooked. Always cook your meat. Raw meat has a 25% chance to give you dysentery (causes rapid thirst drain for 3 minutes). Cooked meat eliminates this risk entirely.

Build Priority #2: Workbench Level 1 (Unlocks Everything)

The workbench is the crafting hub of Windrose. Until you place one, your crafting menu only shows primitive recipes (stone tools, basic shelter). The workbench unlocks 30+ new recipes including better tools, primitive armor, storage containers, and upgrade materials. Workbench Lv1 recipe: 10 wood + 5 stone + 3 plant fiber. Place it under a roof or at least under dense tree cover — rain can slow crafting speed if the workbench is exposed.

Once your workbench is placed, interact with it to see the full recipe list. The most important early unlocks are: Stone Pickaxe (2 wood + 3 stone + 1 fiber — mines ore nodes), Survivor Boots (2 coarse leather + 3 plant fiber — +10% movement speed), Wooden Shield (5 wood + 2 plant fiber — essential for combat), and Rope (3 plant fiber — used in 20+ recipes).

Build Priority #3: Cooking Fire Pit (Upgrade from Campfire)

The cooking fire pit is an upgraded campfire that can cook 3 items simultaneously and has a 20% faster cook time. Recipe: 8 wood + 4 stone + 2 plant fiber. This structure is not mandatory in hour 1-3, but if you have the materials, build it. The efficiency gain is worth it once you start cooking large amounts of meat for the coming days.

Build Priority #4: Tent / Bedroll (Sleep Skip)

A bedroll (5 cloth + 3 wood) or tent (15 cloth + 10 wood + 5 plant fiber) allows you to sleep through the night. Sleeping until dawn restores 50% health and passes time, which is useful when you have done your day's work and want to skip the dangerous nighttime. Cloth is crafted from plant fiber (3 fiber = 1 cloth) at the workbench. Build a bedroll as soon as you have 5 cloth. Place it near your campfire — the "near a lit campfire" buff gives you a 20% rest effectiveness bonus.

Build Priority #5: Armor Workbench (Requires Roof)

The armor workbench unlocks leather and reinforced armor recipes. IMPORTANT RULE: the armor workbench can ONLY be placed under a roof. You must build a 2x2 or 3x3 roof section before you can place the armor workbench. This is a common stumbling block for new players who try to place it in the open. Armor Workbench recipe: 12 wood + 8 stone + 4 plant fiber. Once placed, you can craft Coarse Leather Armor (chest: 4 leather + 3 fiber, legs: 3 leather + 2 fiber, boots: 2 leather + 2 fiber). A full set gives +30 armor rating, reducing all physical damage by 15%.

⚔ 4. Hour 3-6: First Combat & Armor Crafting (Soulslite Combat Primer)

By hour 3, you have a campfire, a workbench, basic tools, and probably some coarse leather armor pieces. Now it is time to engage with Windrose's combat system — a soulslite (souls-like + lighter) system that rewards patience, pattern reading, and stamina management over button mashing. If you have played Dark Souls, Elden Ring, or Lies of P, you already know the fundamentals. If not, this section will teach you everything you need to survive your first fights.

Stamina Management — The Invisible Resource Bar

Every combat action in Windrose consumes stamina: light attack (15 stamina), heavy attack (30 stamina), dodge (20 stamina), and block (10 stamina per hit absorbed). Your stamina bar is the green meter under your health bar. If it hits zero, you cannot attack, dodge, or block for 3 seconds — during which you are completely vulnerable. Rule #1 of Windrose combat: never let your stamina hit zero. Always leave at least 20 stamina in reserve for an emergency dodge. After you attack, retreat and wait for your stamina to regenerate to at least 60% before engaging again. This "hit-and-retreat" rhythm is the foundation of all Windrose combat.

Dodge Frames — Your Invincibility Window

When you press Spacebar + direction to dodge, your character has approximately 0.4 seconds of invincibility frames (i-frames) at the start of the dodge animation. During these frames, enemy attacks pass through you without dealing damage. The timing window is more forgiving than Dark Souls but tighter than God of War. Practice dodging against boars — their charge attack is slow and telegraphed, making it the perfect training dummy. Dodge INTO the attack (toward the boar) rather than away for maximum i-frame utilization. Dodging away is predictable and can still get you clipped by the attack's hitbox tail.

Perfect Block — The Game-Breaker

If you block an attack at the exact moment it would land (approximately 0.2 seconds before impact), you perform a perfect block. This staggers the enemy for 2 seconds and leaves them open to a critical hit that deals 200% damage. The visual cue is a white flash on your shield at the moment of impact. Perfect blocks do not consume stamina. This is the single most powerful mechanic in the game for new players because it allows you to turn aggressive enemies into punching bags. Practice the timing against skeleton pirates (found near beach ruins) — their slow sword swings are perfect for learning the window.

Hit-and-Run Tactics — The Safe Way to Kill Anything

The safest combat strategy for new players is hit-and-run: (1) Wait for the enemy to finish their attack animation. (2) Step in and land 1-2 light attacks. (3) Immediately retreat before they can attack again. (4) Wait for stamina to regenerate. (5) Repeat. This tactic works on every enemy in the game, including bosses. It is slow, but it is safe. As you get more comfortable with dodge timing, you can be more aggressive, but for the first 10 hours, hit-and-run is your best friend.

Food Buff Stacking — The Hidden Power

Windrose allows you to stack buffs from two different food types simultaneously. This means you can eat a coconut (restores hunger + thirst) and cooked boar meat (restores hunger + minor health regen) at the same time, and both buffs will be active. Smart food combos can give you significant combat advantages. For example: Cooked Boar Meat (health regen) + Roasted Sweet Potato (stamina regen) = you can fight much longer without needing to retreat. Experiment with different food combinations as you discover new recipes. The buff icons appear as small squares to the right of your health bar — mouse over them to see what each one does.

Recommended Early Skill Point Allocation

Windrose uses a skill point system where you allocate points into three trees: Toughguy (survival & defense), Slasher (offense & weapons), and Salty Dog (sailing & exploration). For your first 10 points, prioritize the Toughguy tree. Specifically: Endurance (increases max stamina by 15% per point — take 3 points) and Iron Constitution (increases max health by 10% per point — take 2 points). More stamina means you can attack more times before needing to retreat. More health means mistakes are less punishing. Once you have 5 points in Toughguy, put 3 points into Slasher — Sharp Edge (increases light attack damage by 12%) and Heavy Hitter (increases heavy attack damage by 15%).

Combat Warning — Do Not Get Surrounded: Windrose enemies can aggro in groups. If you see 3 boars, do NOT attack all of them at once. Kite them one by one. If you get surrounded, your only option is to dodge repeatedly while backing away until one enemy breaks aggro. Fighting multiple enemies at once is the #2 cause of early-game death (after sharks).

🌐 5. Hour 6-12: First-Night Safe Base Setup (Location + Defense)

You have survived the first day. You have tools, basic armor, and a campfire. Now you need a proper base — a safe place to store your loot, craft advanced items, and sleep safely through the night. Base building in Windrose is deep and complex, but for your first 12 hours, you only need a simple, functional outpost. Do not try to build a castle on day 1. That is how you run out of materials and get caught outside at night with no shelter.

Base Location Selection — Where to Build

Your first base location will determine your quality of life for the next 20+ hours, so choose wisely. The ideal location has these characteristics: (1) Near fresh water — a river or pond within 50 meters. You will be refilling your water constantly in the early game. (2) Not in a low-lying area — some islands have tidal mechanics. Building in a low area means your base can flood at high tide, ruining storage containers and putting out your campfires. Build on slightly elevated ground. (3) Near a beach — you need to dock your ship. A 2-minute walk from your base to the water is acceptable; a 10-minute walk is not. (4) Away from skeleton spawn points — check the area for bone piles, gravestones, or ruined structures. These indicate enemy spawn zones. You do not want to build next to a skeleton farm.

Best biome for first base: Tropical islands (green on the map). They have flat terrain, abundant wood and fiber, passive wildlife, and no dangerous environmental hazards. Avoid swamp islands (venomous snakes) and volcanic islands (extreme heat damage) for your first base.

Temporary Outpost Strategy — Do Not Overbuild

Many new players make the mistake of building a massive, elaborate base in their first 12 hours. This is a trap. You will discover better building materials, better locations, and better designs as you play. Instead, build a temporary outpost: a 4x4 wooden hut with a campfire, workbench, bedroll, and 2 storage chests. This gives you everything you need without investing massive resources. Once you have explored more of the map and found a truly perfect location, THEN build your "forever base". The temporary outpost can always be repurposed as a resource outpost or dismantled for materials.

Base Defense — Palisade Walls and Traps

Even in the early game, enemies can wander into your base at night. A simple perimeter defense will prevent this. Craft Wooden Palisade segments (5 wood each) and place them in a square around your base. A 6x6 palisade enclosure is enough for your first outpost. The palisade does not make your base impregnable — strong enemies can break it — but it funnels enemies into predictable paths and gives you time to wake up and grab your weapon if you are sleeping. For extra defense, place Wooden Spikes (3 wood + 2 stone each) outside the palisade. Enemies that walk into spikes take 15 damage per second. It is a cheap, effective early warning system.

Rest Buff — The Most Overlooked Mechanic

When you sleep in a bedroll or tent, you get a rest buff that increases all stat regen (health, stamina, hunger) by 30% for 30 minutes. This buff is incredibly powerful and many players never even notice it. To maximize the rest buff duration, place decorative items (anything with the "decoration" tag) near your bed. Each decoration extends the buff duration by 2 minutes, up to a maximum extension of 30 minutes (meaning decorated beds can give up to 60 minutes of rest buff). Rugs, paintings, lanterns, and flower pots all count as decorations. This is why "decorating" your base is not just cosmetic — it has real gameplay benefits.

Death and Item Drops — What You Lose When You Die

One of the most common new player fears is "will I lose everything if I die?" The answer is: no, but with one important exception. When you die in Windrose, you respawn at your last bed or campfire with your FULL inventory intact. Your equipped items and all items in your inventory are safe. However, any items that were dropped on the ground (not in your inventory or a container) when you died will despawn after 10 minutes. Also, if you die while sailing, your ship will remain at your location of death — you may need to swim out to retrieve it. The key takeaway: never leave valuable items on the ground. Always store them in a chest or keep them in your inventory.

CRITICAL: Windrose Has No Pause Function! Unlike many survival games, Windrose does NOT pause when you open your inventory or menu. If you need to step away from your keyboard, you MUST find a safe spot, log out properly (Esc → Quit to Menu), or build a bed and sleep. Simply alt-tabbing or walking away will get your character killed by wandering enemies. This catches many new players off guard — never leave your character unattended.

📚 6. Day 2-3 Advanced: Backpack Upgrade, Cooking System, Rest Buff

By day 2, you have survived at least one full night cycle. You have a small base, basic tools and armor, and a campfire. Now the game opens up. You can start exploring other islands, tackling tougher enemies, and unlocking the systems that make Windrose special. This section covers the three most important upgrades for days 2-3: expanding your inventory, mastering the cooking system, and optimizing your rest buff.

Backpack Upgrade — Torn Sack to Worn Sailcloth Bag

Your starting inventory has 12 slots. This is painfully small. You will constantly be forced to drop items because your bag is full. The first upgrade is the Worn Sailcloth Bag (sometimes called "Torn Cloth Pack" in older guides), which adds 8 additional slots for a total of 20. Recipe: 4 coarse cloth + 3 rope + 2 leather scraps. Coarse cloth is crafted from plant fiber (5 fiber = 1 coarse cloth) at the workbench. Rope is crafted from plant fiber (3 fiber = 1 rope). Leather scraps are obtained by butchering boars or finding them in coastal wreckage crates. Once crafted, equip the bag in your inventory's backpack slot (drag it to the small backpack icon). The inventory size increase is immediate and permanent — you do not need to "re-equip" it after logging out.

There are further backpack upgrades (Reinforced Leather Pack: +12 slots, Canvas Expedition Bag: +20 slots), but these require materials you will not have until day 4-5. Focus on the Worn Sailcloth Bag first. The inventory relief is immediate and makes exploration trips much more productive.

Cooking System — From Raw Meat to Buff Feasts

The cooking system in Windrose is deceptively deep. Different foods provide different buffs, and combining foods (as mentioned in Section 4) lets you stack buffs. Here are the most important early-game recipes you should prioritize:

Roast Boar Meat: Cooked boar meat on a campfire. Restores 40% hunger. Provides minor health regen (+3 HP/sec for 3 minutes). This is your bread-and-butter food for the first 10 hours. Always carry 3-5 pieces.

Roasted Coconut: Cooked coconut on a campfire. Restores 25% hunger AND 20% thirst simultaneously. The only early food that restores both. Essential for long exploration trips where you may not find a water source.

Fish Stew: Cooked at a Cooking Fire Pit (upgrade from campfire). Requires 1 raw fish + 1 coconut + 1 herb. Restores 60% hunger, 30% thirst, and provides +15% stamina regen for 5 minutes. This is the best early-game combat food because the stamina regen lets you attack more aggressively.

Herb Roasted Meat: Cooked boar meat + any herb at the Cooking Fire Pit. Restores 50% hunger and provides +20% damage resistance for 4 minutes. Excellent for boss fights and dangerous areas.

Rest Buff Optimization — Sleep Your Way to Power

We briefly mentioned the rest buff in Section 5, but it deserves a deeper explanation because it is one of the most impactful mechanics in the game. The rest buff is applied when you sleep in any bed for at least 30 seconds (you do not need to skip the full night). The base buff duration is 30 minutes and provides: +30% health regen, +30% stamina regen, +15% hunger drain resistance (you get hungry 15% slower). These bonuses stack multiplicatively with food buffs, meaning a well-rested, well-fed character is dramatically more capable than a tired, hungry one.

To extend the rest buff, place decorative items near your bed. Each decoration adds +2 minutes, up to +30 minutes maximum. A fully decorated bedroom gives you a 60-minute rest buff — that is, the buff lasts for the entire in-game day. Since decorations are cheap (rugs cost 2 cloth, lanterns cost 3 wood + 1 fiber), there is no reason not to decorate your bedroom immediately. This is one of the highest "return on investment" activities in early Windrose.

Experience Gain — How to Level Up Fast

You gain experience in Windrose by: (1) killing enemies, (2) harvesting resources, (3) crafting items, and (4) completing quests given by NPCs (found on some islands). The fastest early-game XP comes from clearing POIs (Points of Interest). A POI is any marked location on your map — ruins, caves, shipwrecks, abandoned camps, etc. Enter the POI, kill all enemies, loot all chests, and the POI is "cleared". Cleared POIs give a large XP reward (50-100 XP depending on size) and often contain rare loot like flintlock pistols, blueprints, and upgrade materials. One full POI clear is worth 30 minutes of casual resource gathering.

Respec is FREE: Unlike many games that charge gold or items to respec your skills, Windrose lets you reallocate all your skill points at any bed or campfire for zero cost. If you put points into the wrong tree or want to try a different build, just interact with your bed and select "Respec". This freedom means you should experiment with different builds early on. Try a tanky Toughguy build, then respec into a glass-cannon Slasher build. See what feels fun. You are not locked into any choice.

Day 2 Goal Checklist: (1) Craft Worn Sailcloth Bag (+8 inventory slots), (2) Build Cooking Fire Pit, (3) Clear 2 POIs near your base, (4) Allocate 5 skill points into Toughguy tree, (5) Cook and eat a Fish Stew for the stamina buff, (6) Decorate your bedroom with at least 5 decorations for max rest buff duration. If you complete all 6, day 2 is a massive success.

💡 7. 3 Life-Saving Secrets (That Competitors Never Mention)

Every major Windrose guide covers the same basic tips: "punch trees", "build a campfire", "don't fight sharks". Those are fine, but they will not save you in the situations that actually kill experienced players. The three secrets below are mechanics that the game does not explain well, that most guides forget to mention, and that can genuinely save your character's life in specific scenarios. These are the kinds of details that separate players who "kind of get by" from players who truly master the game.

Secret #1: Recipe Hidden Unlock Rule — Collect It First!

This is, without question, the most common source of confusion for new Windrose players. You check a guide, see a recipe for a "Flintlock Pistol", go to your workbench, and... it is not there. You have the materials. You have the workbench level. But the recipe is missing. This is NOT a bug. This is NOT a glitch. This is an intended mechanic that the game never explains.

The rule: many recipes in Windrose are HIDDEN by default. They only appear in your crafting menu after you have personally collected (picked up, harvested, or looted) at specific material at least once. The game calls this the "Discovery Crafting System". The idea is to encourage exploration — you find a weird item on the beach, pick it up, and suddenly new recipes appear. In practice, it just confuses new players who follow guides.

Here are the most commonly "missing" recipes and the items you need to collect to unlock them: Flintlock Pistol: need to pick up 1 "Rusted Mechanism" (found in shipwreck chests). Leather Backpack Upgrade: need to collect 1 "Tanned Hide" (crafted from coarse leather at a tanning rack — but you need to pick up the tanned hide item itself). Iron Tools: need to mine 1 iron ore node (grey rock with orange speckles). Ghost Ammo: need to loot 1 "Ghost Essence" from a ghost ship.

If a recipe is not showing up in your menu, ask yourself: "have I EVER held this material in my inventory?" If the answer is no, go find that material. Pick it up. The recipe will appear instantly. This one tip alone will save you hours of frustration and forum searching.

Secret #2: Clam Pearls Work as Emergency Ammo

You are exploring a cave. Your sword breaks. Your pistol is out of bullets. You have no food, low health, and a skeleton pirate is charging you. Most players panic and die here. But if you know this secret, you survive.

When you dig up clams on the beach (look for small mounds in the sand near the waterline, interact with E), you get Clam Pearls (2-4 per clam). These pearls are not just a crafting material — they are also a throwable weapon. Equip the pearls in your hotbar, select them, and right-click to throw. Clam pearls deal 15 damage and have a 10% chance to stun the enemy for 1.5 seconds. The throw range is about 15 meters — not great, but enough to stun a pursuer while you escape.

More importantly, clam pearls can be loaded into certain pistols as improvised ammunition. If you have a flintlock pistol but no lead balls, open your inventory, combine 1 clam pearl + 1 pistol in the crafting menu, and you get "Improvised Pistol Shot" (damage: 60% of normal, but it works). This has saved countless runs where I ran out of proper ammo mid-dungeon. Always carry 5-10 clam pearls as backup ammo. They weigh almost nothing and could save your life.

Secret #3: Right-Click the Map to Place Custom Markers

Windrose's map (M key) is functional but minimal. It shows your position, island outlines, and POI markers. What it does NOT show prominently is the custom marker system, which is incredibly powerful for navigation and resource management.

To place a custom marker: open your map, find a location (you can place markers on any visible area, even if you have not explored it yet), and right-click. A context menu appears with marker options: Resource Node (green icon — use this for berry bushes, good wood nodes, or mining spots you want to return to), Danger (red icon — use this for enemy-heavy areas or hazardous terrain), Base/Camp (blue icon — mark your base or useful outposts), and Custom Note (yellow icon — lets you type a short text note like "good iron here" or "skeleton spawn at night").

Custom markers are visible on your in-game compass (the small bar at the top of the screen) when you are within approximately 200 meters of them. This means you can mark a resource node, walk away to do other things, and then use the compass to navigate back to it later without getting lost. For players who like to explore systematically rather than wandering aimlessly, this feature is indispensable. It is also shared in multiplayer — if you are playing co-op, your markers are visible to your teammates, making coordination much easier.

Bonus Secret — The "Sprint-Jump" Exploit: If you sprint (Shift) and jump (Spacebar) at the same time, you travel about 20% farther than a normal jump. This is not an intended mechanic, but the developers have not patched it out (as of Early Access update 0.7.2). Use it to cross gaps, reach high ledges, or escape enemies. It is particularly useful for reaching POI areas that seem inaccessible without building ramps. Just be careful — fall damage in Windrose is real, and a sprint-jump off a cliff is still fatal.

❓ FAQ — Common Beginner Questions

Q: What difficulty should I choose as a complete beginner?

Choose Calm Waters. The reduced enemy damage and increased resource yield give you room to learn the mechanics without constant frustration. You can always start a new game on High Seas later. There is no shame in playing on the easiest difficulty — the goal is to have fun, not to prove you can beat the hardest mode on your first try.

Q: Can I change the difficulty after I start my game?

No. Difficulty is locked at world creation and cannot be changed. The option is disabled in the settings menu. If you want to play on a different difficulty, you must create a new world. This is why choosing the right difficulty before you start (as explained in Section 1) is so important.

Q: How do I heal in Windrose?

Health regenerates slowly over time (1 HP per 3 seconds) when your hunger and thirst are above 50%. For faster healing: (1) eat cooked food with health regen buffs (cooked boar meat, herb-roasted meat), (2) rest (sleep in a bed for the rest buff, which increases regen to 4 HP per second), (3) use bandages (crafted from 3 plant fiber — heals 30 HP instantly but has a 30-second cooldown). There are no "healing potions" in the early game — food and rest are your primary healing sources.

Q: Can I pause the game?

No. Windrose does not have a pause function. The world continues to simulate even when you have your inventory open or are in a menu. If you need to step away, either log out (Esc → Quit to Menu) or find a safe location and sleep in a bed. Never leave your character unattended in an unsafe area.

Q: What happens when I die? Do I lose my items?

You respawn at your last bed or campfire with your FULL inventory intact. You do NOT drop a loot bag or lose items. However, any items dropped on the ground (not in your inventory or a container) will despawn after 10 minutes. Also, if you die while sailing, your ship stays at the location of your death — you may need to swim out to retrieve it. Dieing is annoying but not catastrophic in Windrose.

Q: How do I increase my inventory size? The starting bag is too small.

Craft backpack upgrades at the workbench. The first upgrade is the Worn Sailcloth Bag (4 coarse cloth + 3 rope + 2 leather scraps) which adds 8 slots. Further upgrades require tanning stations and rarer materials, but the first upgrade alone makes a huge difference. Always prioritize crafting it on day 2.

Q: What is the best early-game food?

Coconuts are the best early food because they restore both hunger AND thirst. For combat situations, cooked boar meat is best (health regen buff). For exploration, roasted coconut is best (restores both needs). Once you unlock the Cooking Fire Pit, Fish Stew is the best all-around food (restores both needs + stamina regen buff).

Q: I put skill points into the wrong tree. Can I respec?

Yes! Respecing is completely FREE. Interact with any bed or campfire and select "Respec Character". All your skill points are refunded and you can reallocate them however you want. There is no limit to how many times you can respec. Experiment freely with different builds.

Q: Why are some recipes not showing up in my crafting menu even though I have the materials?

This is the "Discovery Crafting" system. Many recipes are hidden until you pick up a specific material for the first time. Check Section 7, Secret #1 for the full explanation. The short answer: go find and pick up the primary material for that recipe. Once it enters your inventory, the recipe will appear.
