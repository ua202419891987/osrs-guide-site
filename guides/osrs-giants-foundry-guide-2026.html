1. Introduction — Why Giants Foundry Changes Smithing

Giants Foundry is a members-only Smithing minigame released in January 2024. It completely redefines how you train Smithing from level 15 onward. Unlike Blast Furnace which costs millions in gold ore, or anvil smithing which burns through bars for slow XP, Giants Foundry lets you convert metal bars into giant swords through a fun, interactive process that actually engages you rather than putting you to sleep. Before Giants Foundry, the Smithing skill was widely considered one of the most tedious skills in OSRS — expensive, repetitive, and unrewarding. The Foundry changed all of that.

The key advantage is simple: Giants Foundry is either profitable or break-even at most levels. You get excellent XP rates while losing minimal GP — or even making money. For ironmen, this is the single best Smithing method in the game, hands down. For mains, it competes directly with Blast Furnace once you factor in the massive cost savings. When you consider that gold ore at Blast Furnace costs around 500-700K GP per hour to train, Giants Foundry's break-even nature means you effectively save millions over the course of 80-100 hours of training.

Beyond cost, the minigame is genuinely engaging. The hammering mechanics, alloy system, and quality scoring give you meaningful choices with every sword you forge. Compare that to anvil smithing where you click the same platebody 10,000 times, or Blast Furnace where you spam-click gold ore for hours on end. Giants Foundry respects your time and your attention by offering varied, skill-based gameplay that rewards practice and precision. Each sword is different, and improving your quality score from 70 to 90+ is a satisfying skill progression.

Before we dive into the details, one important clarification: Giants Foundry is not for making specific Smithing products like dart tips, arrowheads, or cannonballs. If your goal is to produce items for profit or for other skills, traditional anvil smithing is still the way to go. But if your goal is Smithing XP — efficiently, cost-effectively, and enjoyably — Giants Foundry is the undisputed best method in OSRS as of 2026.

2. Requirements — What You Need Before Starting

The barrier to entry is refreshingly low. You only need two things, and one of them is basically free:

  • Sleeping Giants quest — A short members quest taking about 10 minutes. No combat is required at all. Start by speaking to The Forgotten Smith at the Sleeping Giants camp, located north-east of Mount Karuulm. The quest involves finding Kovac's notes scattered around the camp, fixing the crucible by retrieving replacement parts from a nearby crate, and lighting the furnace with a tinderbox. The rewards include 1,000 Smithing XP and permanent access to the Giants Foundry minigame. There are no monsters to fight, no puzzles to solve, and no items to bring — just a few clicks and conversations. The quest is fully voice-acted and the lore about the forgotten giants is genuinely interesting for players who enjoy OSRS storytelling.
  • 15 Smithing — This is the bare minimum level to start. You can achieve 15 Smithing through quest rewards alone. The most efficient route: complete The Knight's Sword quest (12,725 Smithing XP, which takes you from 1 to 29 Smithing instantly with zero training). This quest involves helping the Falador white knights find a lost recipe — it requires level 10 Mining to start but no combat. The quest items are easy to obtain: a picture of a sword (bought from the Falador General Store), a redberry pie (can be bought from the Cooks' Guild or made), and an iron bar (easily smelted). If you have already done The Knight's Sword, you can use Client of Kourend (1,000 XP) and the Sleeping Giants quest itself (1,000 XP) for a quick boost. If you cannot do The Knight's Sword yet, simply smelt 200-300 bronze bars at the Lumbridge furnace, or complete Doric's Task for 1,300 XP — this takes about 20 minutes of mining and smelting tin and copper at the location north of Falador.
  • Metal bars — You need bars as input material for forging swords. Bronze, iron, steel, mithril, adamantite, and runite bars all work. You can also use ore directly if you have an ore bag, but bars are more efficient. Higher-tier bars yield better quality swords and more XP per commission. We recommend stocking up on at least 500 bars before your first session to avoid constant bank trips.
  • Ice gloves or food — Ice gloves are strongly recommended. They prevent damage when handling hot metal at the crucible, furnace, and water trough. Without them, you take 1-8 damage per interaction. If you do not have ice gloves yet, bring at least monkfish or better to sustain through the damage. You can get ice gloves from the chest in the Heroes' Guild basement after completing Heroes' Quest (requires 55 Fishing, 53 Herblore, and 57 Mining), or simply buy them for 250 GP on the Grand Exchange. For the cost of one sword commission, you save yourself hundreds of HP worth of damage.

4. How Giants Foundry Works — The Full Process Explained

The minigame follows a straightforward loop that takes about 2-4 minutes per cycle. Every commission tasks you with forging a giant sword to a specific shape using a specific mould. Here is the step-by-step breakdown from start to finish:

  1. Take a commission — Talk to The Forgotten Smith and select "Take a commission" from the dialogue options. You will receive a sword mould (which determines the sword shape) and a target quality score. Higher-tier moulds require more bars but yield significantly better XP per commission. You can reject a commission if you do not like the mould type, but you will lose a small amount of reputation — usually not worth it unless you are really aiming for a specific mould.
  2. Choose your alloy — Open the crucible interface by clicking on the large crucible next to the furnace. Select which metals to combine. You can use any combination of bronze through runite bars, or ore if you have an ore bag. The alloy composition directly determines the base quality of your sword, which affects both XP and reputation rewards. The alloy system is the deepest strategic element of the minigame — we cover the best combinations in detail in Section 5.
  3. Load the crucible — Deposit your chosen bars into the crucible through the interface. Each commission has a specific bar capacity shown on the mould. A Basic Mould holds 14 bars, a Double Mould holds 28 bars. Fill the crucible to the maximum capacity for the best results — using fewer bars than the maximum reduces your potential quality score.
  4. Pour the metal — Once the bars are loaded and the crucible is hot enough, click the crucible to pour the molten alloy into your mould. The mould captures the rough shape of the blade. If you poured multiple metal types, the alloy visually blends in the crucible — a nice detail that lets you see your creation come to life.
  5. Hammer the sword — Walk to the anvil (just a few steps from the crucible) and click it. A hammering interface appears showing a cross-section view of your sword. The interface displays multiple segments of the blade, each with its own width and edge properties. Click segments to select them, then choose "Flatten" (widen), "Thin" (narrow and sharpen), or "Round" (balance). The goal is to match the target shape shown at the top of the interface. Each action uses one hammer tick — you have a limited number of ticks per commission, so efficiency matters.
  6. Heat and cool the blade — Use the furnace to reheat specific segments of the blade, making them softer and easier to shape. Use the water trough to cool segments down, hardening them. Hot metal deforms more with each hammer blow but is more controllable. Cold metal barely changes when hammered. The skill comes in maintaining the right temperature profile across the entire blade — too hot and you risk warping; too cold and you waste hammer ticks achieving nothing.
  7. Polish and finish — After hammering to your satisfaction, use the grinding wheel to polish the blade. This final step removes surface-level imperfections and gives a small quality bonus. Always polish before submitting — it only takes one click and adds 10% to your quality score.
  8. Temper the blade — Dip the finished sword into the water trough one final time to quench it. This locks in the shape and adds another small quality bonus. The quenching animation is satisfying and signals that your work is complete.
  9. Submit the sword — Bring the finished sword back to The Forgotten Smith and select "Submit my work." You receive Smithing XP and Foundry Reputation based on the final quality score. The higher the quality, the better the rewards. A well-forged sword at 90+ quality gives nearly double the XP of a rushed 60-quality sword.

A single commission takes about 2-4 minutes depending on your familiarity with the interface, the mould size, and the complexity of the target shape. With practice, you can complete a Basic Mould sword in under 2 minutes and a Double Mould sword in about 3-3.5 minutes. The faster and more accurate your work, the higher the XP per hour.

5. Alloy System — Understanding Metal Tiers and Ratios

The alloy system is the most important strategic element of Giants Foundry. You combine two different metals in the crucible to create an alloy. The alloy's tier and the ratio of metals determine the base quality of the sword, which directly affects XP and reputation rewards. This is where the real optimization of the minigame happens.

Each metal has a numerical tier value: Bronze = 1, Iron = 2, Steel = 3, Mithril = 4, Adamantite = 5, Runite = 6. When you combine two metals, the alloy tier is calculated as the weighted average of the two metals' tiers based on how many bars of each you use. A 50/50 split of two metals gives exactly the midpoint. A 75/25 split leans toward the dominant metal but still benefits from the secondary metal.

The key insight: combining two different metals always gives a bonus over using a single metal type. A pure mithril sword (14 bars of mithril) scores significantly lower than a 7 mithril + 7 adamantite mix, even though both use 14 total bars. The alloy bonus scales with how different the two metals are — combining mithril (4) and adamantite (5) gives a good bonus, but combining iron (2) and runite (6) gives an even larger alloy bonus relative to the average tier. This means you should always, always use a mix of two metals rather than a single type.

Advanced alloy tip: The exact ratio matters. For a 28-bar Double Mould, use exactly 14 of each metal for a perfect 50/50 split. Three-metal alloys? Some players wonder if you can use three different metals in the crucible for an even better alloy. Unfortunately, the game only lets you combine two types of bars at a time in the crucible interface. You select exactly two metals, choose the ratio, and pour. You cannot add a third metal mid-forge or create a triple-alloy. Stick to two metals per commission for the best results.

Alloy tier and Smithing level relationship: Your Smithing level determines which bars you can create, which in turn determines which alloys you can use. At level 15, you can only use bronze and iron (maximum alloy tier 1.5-2.0). At level 30, you unlock steel bars (tier 2.0-3.0 with mithril). At level 50, mithril bars become available (tier 3.0-4.0 alloys). At level 70, adamantite bars unlock (tier 4.0-5.0). At level 85, runite bars unlock (tier 5.0-6.0). Plan your progression around these breakpoints — the jump from steel to mithril (level 30-50) and from mithril to adamantite (level 50-70) are the two biggest XP rate increases in the journey to 99.

Theoretical max XP: The absolute maximum XP per hour at Giants Foundry comes from using a Double Fixed Mould with a 50/50 mix of Adamantite + Runite bars at 99 Smithing, achieving 95+ quality on every sword, while wearing the full Grotesque Armour set and Smithing cape. This combination yields approximately 110K-115K XP/hr. In practice, very few players sustain this rate — most settle into a comfortable 85-100K XP/hr with mithril+adamantite and 85+ quality, which is still excellent training.

7. Sword Shape Mechanics — How to Hammer Your Sword to Perfection

The hammering interface is where your skill as a smith truly matters. This is where high-quality swords are separated from mediocre ones. Here is exactly how the mechanics work and how to master them:

The sword is displayed as a cross-section view on your screen. Each segment of the blade has three adjustable properties: Width (the thickness of that segment), Edge (the sharpness of that segment), and Heat (the current temperature, ranging from cold to white-hot). You must match a target profile shown at the top of the screen, which displays the ideal width and edge for each segment.

  • Hammer actions: Click a segment of the sword to select it, then choose one of three actions: "Flatten" — spreads the metal out, increasing width and decreasing edge sharpness; "Thin" — narrows the segment, decreasing width and increasing edge sharpness; "Round" — balances both properties, slightly increasing width and slightly decreasing edge. Each action consumes one hammer tick, and the total available ticks vary by commission size.
  • Heat management mechanics: The furnace adds heat to a segment, making it softer and more responsive. The water trough removes heat, hardening the segment. Hot segments (orange to white) change significantly with each hammer blow — good for major adjustments but risky because you can easily overshoot. Cold segments (blue to grey) barely change at all — good for fine-tuning but frustrating if you need a big shape change. The ideal working temperature is orange-hot.
  • Visual feedback system: Green indicator arrows next to a segment mean it is within 5% of the target — perfect, leave it alone. Yellow means you are in the right ballpark but need minor adjustments (10-20% off). Red means the segment is significantly off-target (more than 20% off). The game also shows a semi-transparent overlay of the target shape so you can visually compare.
  • Heat transfer between segments: Adjacent segments transfer heat to each other. If you overheat segment 3, segment 2 and 4 will also warm up noticeably. This means you cannot treat each segment in isolation — you must manage the entire temperature profile of the blade. A common mistake is overheating one stubborn segment and then realizing the adjacent segments have become too hot to control.
  • Quenching (tempering): Once your shape is close to perfect, dip the entire blade in the water trough to quench it. This instantly sets the shape — further hammering will have minimal effect. Quenching adds a small quality bonus for proper heat treatment. Then use the grinding wheel to polish — this removes surface-level scratches and gives another quality bonus.
  • Over-hammering penalty: If you continue hammering a segment that is already within the green (matching the target), you will degrade it. The segment will start moving away from the target shape and the quality penalty accumulates. Learn to stop when the indicators turn green. A "less is more" approach almost always scores higher than aggressive hammering.
  • Hammer tick budget: Each commission gives you a limited number of hammer actions. For a Basic Mould (14 bars), you get about 20-25 ticks. For a Double Mould (28 bars), you get 35-40 ticks. This means you cannot endlessly fine-tune — you must prioritize. The most efficient strategy is to get every segment to at least yellow (close) within your first 10 ticks, then spend the remaining ticks perfecting the worst-offending segments.

Pro tip: Focus on getting the width correct first — it accounts for roughly 70% of the shape accuracy score. Edge sharpness matters but is secondary. Once width is within the green on every segment, fine-tune the edge with light "Round" actions. Most beginners over-complicate the process; getting width right on every segment already puts you in the 80th percentile for quality. Master width first, then add edge precision as a secondary skill.

Segment prioritization with Double Moulds: When working with a Double Mould (28 bars, more segments), you cannot afford to spend equal time on every segment. Prioritize the middle segments first — they have the most impact on the overall weapon profile. The end segments (tip and pommel area) have less weight in the quality calculation. If you are running low on hammer ticks, focus on getting the middle segments perfect and accept "good enough" on the ends.

Speedrunning tips for experienced forgers: Once you have forged 50+ swords and consistently score 85-90 quality, you can start optimizing for speed. Key techniques: pre-heat the crucible before starting the commission dialogue; use keyboard shortcuts for hammer actions if available (some third-party clients support this); maintain a consistent three-stroke rhythm per segment — heat, flatten twice, move on. With practice, you can reduce a Double Mould commission from 3.5 minutes to under 3 minutes while maintaining 85+ quality.

8. Quality Score System — How Quality Determines Your Rewards

The quality score is the single factor determining how much XP and reputation you earn per commission. It ranges from 0 to 100 and is calculated from four weighted components. Understanding each component and how to maximize it is the key to high XP rates:

FactorWeightHow to Maximize
Alloy Tier~50%Use the highest-tier metal combination you can access. Mithril + Adamantite (alloy tier 4.5) gives a much higher base than Iron + Steel (2.5). At level 70+, mithril+adamantite is the minimum. This component is purely a function of your Smithing level and bar budget.
Shape Accuracy~30%Precision hammering to match the target profile. Every segment within 5% of the target width gives top marks. The game tracks the deviation of each segment from the target and averages them. Practice makes perfect — the hammering becomes intuitive after about 10-15 swords.
Heat Management~10%Avoid overheating any segment beyond white-hot. Maintain consistent heat across all segments. Quench at the right moment: too early and the shape is not fully set; too late and you lose the bonus entirely. Quench when you have at least 80% of segments in the green.
Polishing~10%Always polish at the grinding wheel before submitting. This step only adds points — there is no downside. A single pass across the grinding wheel is enough for the full 10% bonus. Never skip this step.

Quality score thresholds (what they mean for your rewards): Below 60 = poor quality — you earn roughly 60% of the base XP and reputation. 60-79 = good quality — you earn about 80% of the maximum possible XP. 80-89 = excellent quality — you earn about 90% of the max. 90-100 = near-perfect to perfect — you earn 95-100% of the maximum. Once you consistently hit 80+ quality, you are doing better than the vast majority of players.

XP formula insight: The XP reward is calculated as base_xp x quality_multiplier x mould_multiplier. The base XP depends on the alloy tier and the total number of bars used. Higher alloy tier and more bars = higher base XP. The quality_multiplier is 0.5 + (quality_score / 200). So a 90 quality sword gives a multiplier of 0.5 + 0.45 = 0.95x of base XP, while a 60 quality sword gives 0.5 + 0.3 = 0.8x. The mould_multiplier is 1.0x for Basic/Fixed and 1.1x for Double/Double Fixed. This is why the Double Mould with a 90+ quality sword consistently gives the best XP in the game.

How reputation is calculated: Reputation follows a similar formula but with a different scaling factor. You earn roughly 40-60 reputation per hour with efficient forging. At 250 reputation for the Double Mould unlock, you need about 5 hours of forging to afford your first major upgrade. The Grotesque Armour set at 1,000 reputation takes about 18-20 hours of forging to unlock completely.

11. Inventory Setup and Banking Tips — Maximizing Session Efficiency

How you set up your inventory and manage your banking directly impacts your XP per hour. Small optimizations add up significantly over a 80-100 hour grind to 99 Smithing. Here are the key efficiency principles:

If you have not unlocked ice gloves, bring 5-10 monkfish per hour for healing. You can store food in your inventory alongside bars, but it slows your withdraw-all at the bank.
  • Empty inventory except bars: Your inventory should contain only the bars you need and ice gloves (if not yet equipped). Everything else stays in the bank. The fewer items you have, the fewer right-click menus you deal with when interacting with the crucible and anvil. A clean inventory means clean clicks and fewer misclicks.

This is useful for ironmen who have ore stockpiled but minimal bars. Raw ore goes into the crucible and the game treats it the same as bars for alloy calculations. The ore bag holds up to 60 ore, enough for multiple commissions.
  • Stamina management: The Foundry camp is compact — you never need to run more than 10 steps between the bank chest, crucible, anvil, furnace, and water trough.   • Food management: If you do not have ice gloves, bring food in the gear you wear (not your inventory). Use the bank chest to withdraw food as needed. Having food in your inventory takes up space that could hold bars for another sword. Consider anglerfish for their healing overheal effect if you plan to take chip damage.
  • Rune pouch and teleports: If you plan to use the minigame teleport to leave and return, bring a rune pouch with house teleport runes or a teleport jewelry piece. This lets you reset your minigame teleport cooldown by leaving via house teleport and returning later.

17. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced players make these mistakes when starting Giants Foundry. Avoid them and your XP rates will be significantly higher:

  • Skipping the Double Mould: Many new players grind to 250 reputation and think "I will save up for the Grotesque Armour first instead." This is a mistake. The Double Mould increases your XP per hour by 60-80% immediately, which means you earn reputation faster for everything else. Buy the Double Mould first, always.
  • Using single-metal alloys: Never use 14 bars of mithril alone. Always mix two different metals. A 7 mithril + 7 adamantite split (tier 4.5) is dramatically better than 14 mithril (tier 4). The alloy bonus for mixing is significant and there is no downside — always mix two metals.
  • Over-hammering: Once a segment indicator turns green (within 5% of target), stop immediately. Continuing to hammer degrades the quality. The most common quality killer is someone who keeps hammering a segment that was already perfect. Trust the indicator and move on to the next segment.
  • Neglecting heat management: Hammering a cold segment is like hitting a brick wall — you waste hammer ticks for minimal change. Keep segments in the orange-to-yellow heat range for efficient shaping. Learn to use the furnace proactively, not reactively when a segment has already cooled.
  • Skipping polish: The grinding wheel takes one click and gives a free 10% quality bonus. There is no reason to skip it, yet many players forget. Make it part of your muscle memory: shape, quench, polish, submit — in that order, every time.
  • Wrong bar ratios: For a 14-bar commission, use exactly 7 of each metal. For a 28-bar Double Mould, use exactly 14 of each. Uneven ratios reduce the alloy bonus. The alloy system rewards perfect balance.
  • Rushing commissions: Going too fast drops your quality score below 80, which reduces XP per sword. The optimal pace is 90 seconds for a Basic Mould, 2-2.5 minutes for a Double Mould. Find your rhythm and stick to it.
  • Not using bank presets: Manually withdrawing bars each trip adds up to hours of wasted time over the 99 journey. Spend 2 minutes setting up a bank preset and save yourself 5+ hours of clicking over 80-100 hours of training.
  • Giving up too early: The first 10-20 swords will feel clumsy and slow. Your quality scores will be in the 50-65 range. This is normal. By sword 30, you will be hitting 75-85 consistently. By sword 100, 90+ quality becomes routine. The learning curve is real but short — do not judge the method by your first hour.
  • Using ice gloves as a crutch for bad heat management: Even with ice gloves, you still need to manage the heat of each segment. Ice gloves prevent player damage; they do not control the sword temperature. Keep checking the segment colors and use the furnace proactively.
  • Forgetting your mould before loading bars: A common newbie mistake: loading bars into the crucible before taking a commission. The crucible interface only shows the alloy options after you have a mould. Always take a commission first, then load bars. If you load bars without a mould, you waste time and cannot proceed.
  • Not knowing the right hammer actions: Each segment type has an ideal action. Tapered segments (wider at one end) respond best to "Round." Symmetrical segments respond best to "Flatten" or "Thin." Learn which action works for each segment shape by experimenting with your first 10 swords.
  • Submitting without checking the full interface: Always scan every segment one last time before quenching and polishing. One missed segment in the red zone can drop your quality score by 10-15 points. A final 5-second scan saves you from a bad score.
  • Buying the wrong bars: Check the Grand Exchange prices before buying. Bar prices fluctuate. Sometimes mithril bars spike, making mithril + adamantite suddenly expensive. Adjust your alloy choice based on current market prices.
  • Forgetting to equip the Grotesque Armour: Once you buy the Grotesque Armour pieces, you must equip them to receive the XP bonus. They do not work from the inventory. The boots, gloves, and legs must all be worn simultaneously for the full 5% set bonus.

  • Buy duplicate weapon appearances: Some players collect multiple copies for different cosmetic setups. Each appearance costs 100-200 reputation. If you want the Frozen, Fiery, and Lightning sword looks for different occasions, stock up.

  • Continue forging for profit: Even at 99 Smithing, Giants Foundry with iron + steel bars is profitable at about 100K-150K GP per hour. If you enjoy the minigame, you can keep forging for GP while watching your XP counter tick over for virtual levels and collection log slots.
  • Help clanmates and friends: You can teach other players how to forge efficiently. The Foundry is best enjoyed with a friend on voice chat — the relaxed pace leaves plenty of room for conversation while staying productive.
  • Nothing required: Unlike some other minigames that have consumable upkeep costs (like GOTR needing guardian essence), Giants Foundry has no ongoing costs once you have unlocked everything. Your rank and mould unlocks are permanent. You can leave and come back at any time without penalty. This makes it a "fire and forget" minigame — once you have what you need, you never have to worry about maintaining it.
  • Flex on your friends: The weapon appearances from Giants Foundry are exclusive and rare. If you see another player wearing a Frozen Sword appearance, you know they put in the hours at the Foundry. It is a subtle but respected badge of Smithing dedication.

21. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What Smithing level should I start Giants Foundry?

A: Level 15 — immediately after completing Sleeping Giants. There is no reason to wait. The minigame scales with your level, and lower-tier alloys like iron + steel are profitable. Starting early means you build up both Smithing XP and Foundry Reputation simultaneously, so by the time you reach 70 Smithing, you already have enough reputation for the Double Mould and part of the Grotesque Armour set.

Q: Can I make GP at Giants Foundry?

A: Yes, noticeably at lower levels. Using iron + steel bars, you profit about 100K-150K GP per hour while training Smithing at 15-40K XP/hr. Even at higher levels, mithril + adamantite is near break-even. This is dramatically better than Blast Furnace, where you lose 500K-700K GP per hour. See our Money Making Guide for comparison against other OSRS money-making methods.

Q: Is Giants Foundry AFK?

A: Not truly AFK, but semi-AFK. Each commission takes 2-4 minutes and requires active attention for hammering, heat management, and polishing. You cannot walk away mid-commission because the minigame pauses the hammering interface but will time out after about 60 seconds. However, the pace is relaxed — it is excellent for second-screen entertainment like watching videos or listening to podcasts.

Q: Do I need ice gloves for Giants Foundry?

A: Highly recommended for long sessions. Without ice gloves, handling hot metal damages you for 1-8 damage per interaction. Over a full training session, you will take 50-100 damage per hour. With ice gloves, you take zero damage. They cost 250 GP on the GE — well worth the investment.

Q: Does the Grotesque Armour outfit stack with the Smithing cape?

A: Yes, they stack additively. The Grotesque Armour set gives 5% Smithing XP bonus. The Smithing cape gives 5% bonus when worn. Combined = 10% additional XP. Both can be worn simultaneously. During bonus XP weekends, these bonuses multiply further, potentially reaching 110K-115K XP/hr at level 99.

Q: How is Giants Foundry for ultimate ironmen (UIM)?

A: Workable but requires planning. UIMs should store bars in a looting bag before starting. The Foundry has a bank chest, but UIMs cannot use banks. The best approach is to bring 28 bars at a time, complete the commission, and drop the rewards to retrieve later. It takes more trips but is still better than anvil smithing. The Grotesque Armour can be stored in your POH costume room.

Q: Can I use raw ore instead of bars?

A: Yes, raw ore works in the crucible. If you have an ore bag from Motherlode Mine, you can load raw ore directly. The game treats raw ore the same as bars for alloy tier calculations. This is especially useful for ironmen who have ore stockpiled. The downside is you miss out on smelting XP, but that is irrelevant if you are here for Smithing XP from forging.

Q: How long to unlock the Double Mould?

A: About 3-5 hours of forging. The Double Mould costs 250 reputation. With a Basic Mould and iron + steel alloy (scoring 70-80 quality per sword), you earn about 12-15 reputation per commission. At 12-18 commissions per hour, you earn 150-250 reputation per hour. You can unlock the Double Mould in a single focused session.

Q: Is Giants Foundry better than Blast Furnace for ironmen?

A: Yes, without question. Blast Furnace requires significant GP for furnace fees (2,500 GP per 28 ore) plus coal costs. Ironmen struggle to sustain this. Giants Foundry requires zero GP — just the bars you have mined or obtained from PvM. It is the single best Smithing method for ironmen at every level.

Q: What is the fastest way to Smithing 99 in OSRS?

A: Blast Furnace with gold ore at 300K XP/hr (very costly). But the most practical path is Giants Foundry 15-80 (profitable), then Blast Furnace 80-99 (if you have GP). The fastest pure method is Blast Furnace gold ore at 300K XP/hr, costing 30M+ GP total. The most balanced method is Giants Foundry the entire way at 80-100K XP/hr with near-zero cost. Choose based on your GP. See our Complete Skill Training Guide for more details.

Q: Can I do Giants Foundry with a group?

A: Yes, but it is not required. Giants Foundry is designed as a solo activity. Each player works on their own commission independently. However, you can be in the same area as other players — the space has multiple anvils, furnaces, and water troughs. There is no competition for resources. Bring a friend and chat while you forge for a more social experience.

Q: What items should I definitely NOT bring to Giants Foundry?

A: Avoid bringing untradeable items you care about losing. The minigame is safe (no death mechanics), but having unnecessary items in your inventory clutters the interface. Leave your pickaxe, axe, fishing rod, and other skilling tools in the bank. You only need bars and ice gloves. Extra items increase right-click menus and slow you down.

Q: How does Giants Foundry compare to making platebodies for profit?

A: Giants Foundry is better for XP and similar for profit. Smithing rune platebodies can be profitable (around 100K-200K GP/hr) but requires 99 Smithing and gives about 60K XP/hr. Giants Foundry at 99 gives 85-100K XP/hr with similar profit potential using iron+steel. For ironmen who need to alch, platebodies may still have a place, but for pure XP training, the Foundry wins.

Q: Do the weapon appearances from Giants Foundry work on all weapons?

A: No, they work on most melee weapons but not all. The Frozen, Fiery, and Lightning sword appearances work on most swords, scimitars, longswords, and similar blade-type weapons. They do not work on daggers, maces, warhammers, or ranged/magic weapons. Check the appearance in your inventory before assuming it works with your gear setup.

Q: Can I use the Giants Foundry on a free-to-play account?

A: No, Giants Foundry is members-only. The Sleeping Giants quest, the minigame, and the entire area require a members subscription. F2P players should stick to anvil smithing with bronze and iron bars at Varrock or Lumbridge. Consider getting a bond if you want access to this training method — the GP savings alone from training here instead of anvil smithing will pay for the bond.

Q: What is the best quality score I should aim for?

A: Consistently 85+ is excellent. Above 90 is near-perfect. When you are learning, aim for 70-75. After 20-30 swords, aim for 80+. Once you have mastered the hammering, you should hit 85-92 regularly. Quality above 95 is possible but requires near-perfect heat management AND shape accuracy — most players never bother chasing 95+ because the XP difference from 92 to 95 is marginal (about 3%).

Q: Does the Giants Foundry have a bank deposit box?

A: No, but it has a full bank chest. Unlike some minigames that only have deposit boxes (like Wintertodt or Motherlode Mine), Giants Foundry has a complete bank chest where you can deposit and withdraw any items. This makes inventory management much easier. You can even change your gear and restock on food or bars without leaving the area.

Q: How do I know how much reputation I have?

A: Check the rewards shop interface or talk to The Forgotten Smith. Your current Foundry Reputation is displayed at the top of the rewards shop screen when you browse available items. You can also ask The Forgotten Smith about your progress, and he will tell you your current reputation total and how many commissions you have completed.

Q: Is there a collection log for Giants Foundry?

A: Yes, there is a collection log slot for Giants Foundry items. The collection log tracks unique items you unlock from the rewards shop, including each Grotesque Armour piece and each weapon appearance. If you are a collection log hunter, Giants Foundry is a relatively easy slot to fill — all items are bought with reputation and have no RNG involved. You are guaranteed every item by the time you have enough reputation.

Q: Can I lose items if I die at Giants Foundry?

A: No, Giants Foundry is a safe minigame. There is no death mechanic at the Foundry. If you take damage from hot metal and your HP reaches 0, you will respawn at the nearby respawn point (the Sleeping Giants camp entrance) with all items intact. You do not lose anything. This makes it completely safe for Ultimate Ironmen and Hardcore Ironmen alike.

22. Glossary of Key Terms

Here is a quick reference for the terminology used throughout this guide and in the Giants Foundry minigame:

  • Alloy: A mixture of two different metals combined in the crucible. The alloy tier is the weighted average of the two metals' tiers.
  • Alloy Tier: A numerical value from 1.0 to 6.0 representing the quality of your metal mix. Bronze=1, Iron=2, Steel=3, Mithril=4, Adamantite=5, Runite=6.
  • Anvil: The workstation where you hammer your sword into shape. Located a few steps from the crucible.
  • Basic Mould: The default mould for commissions. Holds 14 bars total. No quality multiplier.
  • Commission: A task from The Forgotten Smith to forge a specific sword. Each commission gives a mould and a target shape.
  • Cross-section view: The interface showing the width and edge profile of each segment of your sword, used during hammering.
  • Crucible: The container where you load and melt your metal bars before pouring into the mould.
  • Double Mould: An upgraded mould that holds 28 bars (2x) and gives a 1.1x quality multiplier. Requires 250 reputation to unlock.
  • Double Fixed Mould: The best mould in the game. 28 bars, 1.1x quality multiplier, different target shape. Requires both Double and Fixed moulds.
  • Fixed Mould: A 14-bar mould with a different target shape than Basic. Used for variety and as a prerequisite for Double Fixed.
  • Forgotten Smith: The NPC who gives commissions and accepts completed swords. Located at the center of the Sleeping Giants camp.
  • Foundry Reputation: The currency earned from completing commissions. Spent at the rewards shop for moulds, armour, and cosmetics.
  • Grotesque Armour: A Smithing XP-boosting outfit. Full set gives 5% bonus. Boots (250 rep), Gloves (350), Legs (400).
  • Grinding Wheel: Used to polish the finished sword before submission. Adds 10% to quality score.
  • Hammer Tick: Each hammer action (Flatten, Thin, Round) consumes one tick. Limited number per commission.
  • Ice Gloves: Protective gloves that prevent damage from handling hot metal. Highly recommended.
  • Mould: Determines the sword shape and bar capacity of each commission. Basic, Double, Fixed, Double Fixed.
  • Ore Bag: A bag from Motherlode Mine that lets you carry raw ore. Useful for bringing ore directly to the Foundry.
  • Quality Score: A rating from 0-100 on your finished sword. Determines XP and reputation rewards.
  • Quenching: Dipping the hot blade in water to set the shape and add a quality bonus.
  • Segment: A section of the sword blade in the cross-section view. Each segment has width and edge properties.
  • Sleeping Giants: The quest required to unlock Giants Foundry. Takes 10 minutes, no combat needed. Speak to The Forgotten Smith to start the quest.
  • Water Trough: Used to cool hot metal segments and to quench the finished blade. Located next to the anvil and furnace.
  • Weapon Appearances: Cosmetic overrides (Frozen, Fiery, Lightning, Shadow) that change your weapon's visual look. Purchased with reputation.
  • Width: The thickness of a blade segment in the cross-section view. One of three adjustable properties (width, edge, heat).
  • XP Multiplier: The quality-based multiplier applied to base XP. Formula: 0.5 + (quality / 200). A quality of 90 gives 0.95x base XP.

🔨 Smithing Without the Bank Breaking

Giants Foundry transformed Smithing from a costly, boring grind into a genuinely enjoyable activity that can actually make you money. Whether you are a main account on a tight budget, an ironman working toward your first 99, or a veteran looking for a more engaging Smithing alternative, the Foundry delivers on every front. Complete the Sleeping Giants quest, grab your bars, and start forging. Your Smithing cape awaits — and unlike Blast Furnace, you will not be bankrupt when you get there.

Remember the key points from this guide: use a 50/50 alloy mix of two different metals, prioritize unlocking the Double Mould first, keep your quality score above 80 by managing heat and shape carefully, and always polish before submitting. With these fundamentals mastered, you are on track for the most efficient and enjoyable Smithing training experience in Old School RuneScape.

Good luck on your 99 Smithing journey, and may your swords always be forged at 90+ quality. The forge is hot, the anvil is waiting, and every sword you make is one step closer to 99. See you at the Sleeping Giants camp.
