OSRS Account Hacked Prevention Guide 2026

Losing an Old School RuneScape account you spent years building is devastating — and almost always avoidable. This guide breaks down exactly how accounts get compromised in 2026 and gives you a practical, beginner-friendly checklist to lock yours down today.

🛡️ Level: Beginner
📅 Account Security Series · Part 1 of 2 · 11 min read · Updated July 2026

⏱️ Quick Verdict — 30-Second Read

  • 📌 Phishing is the #1 cause of hacks — fake "you're banned" emails and lookalike login pages, not "super hackers," steal 9 out of 10 accounts.
  • 📌 Turn on 2-Step Verification (2SV) on your Jagex Account with an authenticator app or passkey — it blocks 99% of login attacks even if your password leaks.
  • 📌 Use a unique password you've never reused anywhere else; a free password manager makes this effortless.
  • 📌 Only use official software — the Jagex Launcher and RuneLite from runelite.net. "Cracked" clients and plugins steal your login.
  • 📌 Jagex will never ask for your password — anyone who does is a scammer, every single time.

1. 🔍 How OSRS Accounts Actually Get Hacked

Before you can defend your account, you need to understand what you're defending against. Almost every "my account got hacked" story on the forums comes down to one of five vectors. None of them require the attacker to be a genius — they rely on the player making a small, predictable mistake.

Attack VectorHow It WorksHow to Prevent It
Phishing A fake email, DM, or website tricks you into typing your login on a lookalike page that sends credentials straight to the scammer. Always navigate to the official site yourself; never click login links from messages. Check the URL character-by-character.
Leaked / Reused Password Your password was exposed in a breach of another website (or you reused it), so attackers try it on your Jagex Account. Use a unique, strong password for OSRS only. Enable 2SV so a leaked password alone can't log in.
Malicious Client / Plugin A "better RuneLite," "RuneLite HD crack," or sketchy plugin contains a keylogger or credential stealer. Only use the official Jagex Launcher and RuneLite from runelite.net. Never run "free" premium plugins.
Social Engineering Someone impersonates Jagex Support, a friend, or a giveaway host to talk you into handing over codes or passwords. Jagex never contacts you by DM. Never share 2SV codes or passwords — not even with "friends."
Account Selling / Sharing Letting others "borrow" your account, or buying one, breaks the rules and puts credentials in strangers' hands. Keep your account to yourself. Shared passwords are leaked passwords.
💡 The Uncomfortable Truth

The overwhelming majority of hacks are not sophisticated. They are the digital equivalent of someone finding your house key under the doormat. The defenses in this guide are simple, free, and take about ten minutes total — there is no good reason not to do all of them today.

2. 🎣 Phishing: The #1 Threat & How to Spot It

Phishing is responsible for the vast majority of compromised accounts. The scammer's job is to create urgency or greed so you stop thinking and start clicking. Here are the most common 2026 phishing lures targeting OSRS players:

🪝 Common Phishing Lures

  • "Your account has been banned / locked" — an email or in-game PM with a link to "appeal." The link goes to a fake login page.
  • "You won a bond / rare drop / giveaway" — click here to claim. There is no prize; there is only a credential harvest.
  • "Complete a quick survey for membership" — surveys that ask for your RuneScape login are always scams.
  • Discord / Twitch "mods" and "admins" — impersonators message you about a giveaway or a "verification" step.
  • Lookalike websites — domains like oldschool-runescape[.]com, osrs-gp[.]shop, or anything with a missing/extra letter that mimics the real site.

② How to Spot a Phishing Attempt in 5 Seconds

① Check the URL — slowly

The only official domains are oldschool.runescape.com, runescape.com, jagex.com, and runelite.net. If the address bar shows anything else — even one character off — close the tab immediately.

② Ask: "Did I expect this message?"

Jagex does not email you out of the blue about bans, prizes, or surveys. An unexpected "urgent" message is a red flag by definition.

③ Watch for pressure tactics

"Act within 24 hours or lose your account!" is pure manipulation. Real Jagex communications don't manufacture panic to force a click.

④ Never enter your password on a site you reached via a link

Type the official address into your browser yourself, or use a saved bookmark. If you arrived by clicking, assume the page is hostile.

⚠️ Bookmark the Real Sites

Save oldschool.runescape.com and runelite.net as browser bookmarks and only ever open them from your bookmarks bar. This single habit eliminates the most common phishing failure: "I thought I was on the real site."

3. 🔐 Enable 2-Step Verification (2FA) on Your Jagex Account

In 2026 there is essentially one account system: the Jagex Account. Every modern OSRS login flows through it, and the single most important security setting is 2-Step Verification (2SV). With 2SV on, logging in requires both your password and a second factor — so even if your password is stolen, the attacker is locked out.

2SV MethodStrengthBest For
Authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy, etc.) Very strong — a 6-digit code that changes every 30 seconds Everyone. This is the recommended default.
Passkey (hardware key or device biometrics) Strongest — phishing-resistant by design Players with a phone/security key who want maximum protection.
Email code Moderate — better than nothing, but email can be phished too A fallback if you can't use an app; pair it with a secure email.
No 2SV None — password alone is the only gate Nobody. This is how accounts get taken.

③ How to Turn On 2SV (Step-by-Step)

Step 1

Go to jagex.com and sign in to your Jagex Account. Use your bookmark, not a link from an email.

Step 2

Open Account Settings → Security and choose Set up 2-Step Verification.

Step 3

Pick the authenticator app method, then scan the QR code with your phone's authenticator app. Type the 6-digit code back to confirm it works.

Step 4

Save your backup / recovery codes somewhere offline (a password manager or written on paper). These let you regain access if you lose your phone.

🔑 Don't Skip the Backup Codes

The number-one "I locked myself out" complaint comes from enabling 2SV and then losing the phone with no backup codes saved. Store them now, while you're thinking about it. A screenshot in your photo gallery is not secure enough — use your password manager or paper.

4. 🔑 Password Hygiene: The Foundation

2SV is your vault door, but your password is still the first lock. A weak or reused password is how attackers get the "first factor" in the first place. Good password hygiene is boring, but it is non-negotiable.

④ The Three Rules of a Safe Password

✅ Do This

  • Unique: Never reuse your OSRS password on any other site, game, or forum.
  • Long: Aim for 16+ characters. A random passphrase (e.g. four unrelated words) is both strong and memorable.
  • Stored: Keep it in a password manager so you never have to "remember" it — and never have to write it down insecurely.

❌ Avoid This

  • Reusing a password from a forum, email, or another game (those databases get leaked constantly).
  • Passwords containing your display name, "osrs," "runescape," or birthdays — these are the first things crackers try.
  • Sharing your password with a "friend," a powerleveler, or an account seller.

A free password manager (such as Bitwarden, or the one built into your browser) generates and stores a unique password for every site. You only remember one master password. This single habit protects not just your OSRS account but your email, bank, and everything else.

💡 Your Email Is Part of Your Password

If a scammer resets your Jagex password, the reset link goes to your email. So your email account needs its own unique password and its own 2SV. A secure OSRS account sitting on a throwaway, unprotected email is like a reinforced door with the key left under the mat.

5. 🛡️ RuneLite, the Jagex Launcher & Fake Clients

RuneLite is a beloved, legitimate, free third-party client — but its popularity makes it a prime vehicle for malware. Scammers build "better" versions, "HD cracks," and "premium plugin packs" that are really credential stealers.

① Only download RuneLite from runelite.net

The official site is the only safe source. Any "mirror," "modded RuneLite," or "RuneLite HD download" from a random forum or YouTube description is a trap.

② Use the official Jagex Launcher

Jagex's own launcher is the supported way to run the official client and RuneLite on a Jagex Account. Download it only from jagex.com.

③ Be wary of "free" premium plugins

If a plugin promises paid features for free, or asks you to disable safety settings, walk away. Legitimate RuneLite plugins come through the in-client plugin hub.

④ Keep everything updated

Run the official launcher's updates. Outdated software has known holes that malware exploits.

🚫 "But My Friend Uses It and Is Fine"

Malware is often distributed through compromised accounts and trusted circles precisely because victims lower their guard around people they know. A friend forwarding a "cool client" link is exactly how many infections start. Verify the source yourself at runelite.net.

6. ✅ Your 10-Minute Security Checklist

If you do nothing else today, do these five things. They take about ten minutes and stop the vast majority of real-world attacks:

🎯 The Non-Negotiable Five

  1. Enable 2SV on your Jagex Account with an authenticator app (and save backup codes).
  2. Set a unique 16+ character password for OSRS, stored in a password manager.
  3. Secure your email with its own unique password and 2SV.
  4. Bookmark oldschool.runescape.com and runelite.net; never log in via links.
  5. Uninstall anything you downloaded from a non-official RuneLite source, then reinstall from runelite.net.

Done? Your account is now dramatically safer than the average player's. The remaining risk is social engineering — and the rule for that is simple: Jagex will never ask for your password, your 2SV code, or your bank PIN. Nobody legitimate ever will.

7. 💡 Prevention FAQs

Is the in-game Bank PIN enough to protect my account?

A Bank PIN adds a useful second layer for in-game theft, but it is not a substitute for 2SV. A scammer who controls your login can still change the PIN after waiting out the cooldown, drain your bank, or get you banned through rule-breaking. Treat the Bank PIN as a bonus, not your primary defense — 2SV is the real wall.

I already have a "Jagex Authenticator" from years ago. Is that still good?

Older accounts used the legacy Jagex Authenticator app; modern accounts use 2SV within the Jagex Account system (authenticator app or passkey). If you migrated to a Jagex Account, manage 2SV through Account Settings → Security at jagex.com. If you're unsure what you have, sign in and check the Security page — it will tell you exactly what's active.

Can I get hacked just by clicking a phishing link, without typing anything?

Merely opening a malicious page is rarely enough to take your account — the damage happens when you type your credentials or 2SV code into it. That said, some pages try to load malware, so avoid interacting with anything suspicious. The safe move: close the tab, navigate to the real site yourself, and change your password if you typed anything.

Should I use the same password manager master password everywhere?

No — your password manager has one master password, and that single password plus your 2SV protects everything inside it. Make that master password long and unique, enable the manager's own 2SV, and never reuse it anywhere else. The manager is a vault; the master key to that vault must be strong and exclusive to it.

What if I already shared my password with someone "just once"?

Assume it's compromised. Change your OSRS password immediately, enable 2SV if it isn't on, change your email password too, and check your Jagex Account's "login history" / connected devices for anything unfamiliar. Changing credentials severs anything the other person saved.
