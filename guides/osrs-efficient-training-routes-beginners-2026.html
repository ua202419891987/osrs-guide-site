OSRS Efficient Training Routes for Beginners 2026: Step-by-Step Guide

Published: June9, 2026 | By OSRS Guru Team | Beginner Friendly

Starting your journey in Old School RuneScape can be overwhelming. With 23 skills, hundreds of quests, and countless training methods, knowing where to begin and how to progress efficiently is a challenge every new player faces. This comprehensive OSRS beginner training guide for 2026 provides a step-by-step weekly training route designed specifically for new players. Whether you are completely new to Gielinor or returning after a long break, this guide will help you progress from level 1 to a well-rounded account with clear goals and milestones along the way.

Unlike other guides that simply list training methods, this guide focuses on the sequence of activities that will give you the best return on your time investment. We have structured this as a 4-week beginner training plan, though your actual progression may take longer depending on your playtime. The key to efficiency in OSRS is understanding that certain quests unlock powerful training methods and gear, and that training skills in a specific order can save you hundreds of hours of grinding. By following this structured approach, you will avoid the common pitfalls that cause many new players to quit out of frustration.

Week 1: Foundation Building (Levels 1-30+)

Your first week in OSRS should be focused on understanding the game mechanics, completing essential quests, and building a foundation in the core skills. Do not worry about efficiency or experience rates yet — your goal is to unlock the basic infrastructure that will make your account viable for efficient training later. Start by completing the new player tutorial and claiming your starter items.

Begin your Week 1 journey by completing the following quests in order: cook's Assistant, Daddy's Home, The Knight's Sword (for 12,725 Smithing XP), and Vampyre Slayer (for 4,825 Attack XP). These quests provide substantial experience rewards that will jumpstart your account. Also complete Imp Catcher and Witch's Potion for early Magic levels. By the end of Week 1, you should have several combat skills in the 20-40 range and have unlocked the quest log interface fully.

During Week 1, your training should focus on the following skills in this exact order: Attack, Strength, and Defence to 40 (enables Dragon weapons), Hitpoints will come naturally, Ranged to 30-40 (for Giant assignment), and Magic to 31+ (for teleportation spells). Also train Mining and Smithing enough to make your own armor (around level 20-30 each). Do not worry about Prayer yet — save your bones for later when you have a better altar.

By the end of Week 1, you should have earned your first significant amount of GP (at least 100k+), have combat stats in the 20-40 range, and have a basic understanding of the OSRS user interface. You should also have joined a clan (highly recommended for new players) and started reading the skills overview guide to understand what each skill does. Week 1 is about exploration and learning — don't rush, but do try to follow the quest order to maximize your experience gains efficiently.

Week 2: Combat & Money Making (Levels 30-50+)

Week 2 is where your character starts to take shape. With your combat stats now in the 30-40 range, you can begin tackling more challenging content. This week's focus is on reaching combat stats of 50+ in your chosen attack style, unlocking profitable money-making methods, and beginning your resource gathering skills in earnest. The goal is to become self-sufficient in terms of GP so you can fund your future training.

Your primary combat training for Week 2 should be at Hill Giants (level 28) or Moss Giants (level 42) if your combat stats allow. These monsters drop big bones (for Prayer training later), limpwurt roots (sell for GP), and various herbs. Alternatively, if you have 40 Ranged, you can train on Lesser Nihils or continue with Slayer. Speaking of Slayer, you should complete the The Dwarf Cannon quest ASAP and start Slayer assignments as soon as you unlock the skill at combat level 25+.

Money making in Week 2 should come from a combination of combat loot and gathering skills. Mine Iron Ore in the Mining Guild (level 60+ Mining helps here) and smelt them into iron bars for profit. Alternatively, Cowhide collection and flax picking (then spinning into bowstrings) are excellent low-requirement money makers. As you accumulate wealth, consider buying your first Dragon weapon (Dragon Scimitar is the best melee weapon for a long time). Check our money-making guide for more detailed methods appropriate for your level.

By the end of Week 2, you should have combat stats of at least 50 in your chosen attack style, 40+ in your other combat stats, and enough GP to purchase key upgrades like the Dragon Scimitar (from the Monkey Madness quest) and the Dwarf Multicannon. You should also have started Slayer and earned your first Slayer points. This is also a good time to begin Farming tree runs if you have the GP for tree seeds — even a small investment here pays off tremendously in the long run.

Week 3: Slayer Unlocked (Levels 50-70+)

Week 3 marks a major turning point in your OSRS journey. With Slayer now fully accessible and your combat stats reaching the 50-70 range, you can begin tackling mid-level Slayer tasks that provide excellent combat experience and decent profit. This week is about transitioning from "grinding stats" to "profitable combat training." The Slayer skill is the most important skill for efficient account progression in OSRS.

Your Slayer training should now be your primary focus. Unlock the Slayer Helmet as your first major Slayer reward — it provides a 15% accuracy and damage boost against your current Slayer task. Also unlock Bigger and Badder for a chance at superior Slayer monsters (which drop excellent loot). As you progress through Slayer tasks, you will naturally train all your combat stats simultaneously. Make sure to use the best Magic attacks available to you, such as High Alchemy for profit and combat spells for training.

Week 3 is also the time to begin serious Prayer training if you have been saving bones. Burying big bones gives 15 Prayer XP each, and by now you should have a few hundred from your giant-killing sessions. If you have the GP, consider doing Wintertodt for early Firemaking and some starter GP/consumables. Wintertodt is also excellent for low-level players because it provides substantial rewards even at low combat levels, and it helps train Woodcutting and Firemaking simultaneously.

You should also have started Farming herb runs and possibly begun Crafting some basic gear. The combination of Slayer for combat XP and Farming for passive income is the foundation of every efficient OSRS account. Check our skills progression path guide for more detailed planning.

Week 4: Scaling Up (Levels 70+)

Welcome to Week 4 — the point where your account transitions from "beginner" to "intermediate." With combat stats now in the 60-75 range and several quests completed, you have access to most of the content in OSRS. This week is about optimizing your training methods, setting up efficient daily routines (like Farming runs and Wintertodt runs), and pushing toward your first major milestones like the Quest Cape and Barrows Gloves.

Your primary focus in Week 4 should be optimal Slayer tasks with the best money-making potential. Tasks like Nechryael, Greater Demons, Skeletal Wyverns, and Dust Devils provide excellent profit per hour while still giving good combat experience. Use your Magic and Ranged training through Slayer as well — using the appropriate combat style for each Slayer task is key to efficiency. By now you should also have unlocked the Fighter Torso (from the Barbarian Assault minigame) and the Barrows Gloves (from the Recipe for Disaster quest).

Week 4 is also when you should seriously start training your "secondary" skills — the ones that support your main training. This includes Herblore (for potions), Agility (for run energy reduction and shortcuts), Thieving (for GP and gems), and Construction (for POH teleports). While these skills can be trained later, getting them to decent levels (50-70) early will make all your future training significantly more efficient. Our all skills overview guide has detailed recommendations for the order in which to train these support skills.

You should also have a reliable daily routine set up: Farming runs every day, Wintertodt for rewards, and Slayer as your primary combat training. From here, the game opens up completely — you can pursue bossing, achievement diaries, mini-games, or continue pushing for 99s in your favorite skills. Key Milestones to Track

Tracking your progress through meaningful milestones is one of the best ways to stay motivated in OSRS. Unlike modern MMOs that constantly shower you with rewards, OSRS progression is slow and deliberate. Celebrating these milestones along the way makes the journey much more enjoyable. Here are the most important milestones for a beginner account, in roughly the order you should achieve them.

Quest Cape (all quests completed): This is the single most important milestone for any OSRS account. The Quest Cape grants +1 to all stats (temporary boost) and provides access to virtually all content in the game. It also provides substantial experience rewards across all skills. Our questing guide provides the optimal quest order to achieve this milestone as efficiently as possible. Most players should aim to get their Quest Cape within the first 1-2 months of gameplay.

Combat 70+ (for all combat styles): Having 70 Attack, Strength, Defence, Ranged, Magic, and Prayer opens up the majority of PvM content in OSRS. At these levels, you can effectively tackle Barrows, Fight Caves (if you include 70+ Ranged), and most Slayer tasks. This milestone represents the transition from "learning the game" to "playing the game at full capacity." Your Slayer training will naturally push you toward this milestone if you follow the weekly plans outlined above.

Skills 60+ (for all skills): Having 60 in all skills is another major milestone because it unlocks the Achievement Diary rewards for medium diaries, and it qualifies you for most content in the game. Some skills have specific breakpoints at 60: Farming 60 unlocks the best trees, Mining 60 allows mining in the Mining Guild, and Agility 60 unlocks the Seers' Village course (one of the best Agility courses). Use our all skills overview guide to track your progress toward this milestone.

Barrows Gloves: Obtained by completing the Recipe for Disaster quest (all subquests), Barrows Gloves are the best-in-slot gloves for most melee setups and remain relevant well into the endgame. The quest itself is a massive undertaking that requires significant progression in many skills. However, the rewards are well worth it, and the gloves themselves are a status symbol among OSRS players. This milestone typically takes 1-3 months to achieve for a dedicated player.

First Max Cape (all skills 99): While this is a very long-term goal, it is worth mentioning as the ultimate milestone for any OSRS account. The Max Cape provides numerous benefits and is a testament to your dedication to the game. Most players take 1-3 years of consistent play to achieve this milestone. Don't rush it — enjoy the journey, and remember that bossing and other PvM content can be fun even while you are working toward 99s in various skills.

Common Beginner Mistakes to Avoid

Even with the best training plan, new OSRS players frequently make mistakes that cost them hundreds of hours of progress. Being aware of these common pitfalls can save you an enormous amount of time and frustration. Here are the most important mistakes to avoid, compiled from the experiences of thousands of players who have gone through the beginner phase before you.

Mistake #1: Not following a quest order. Quests provide massive experience rewards and unlock essential content. Doing them in the wrong order means missing out on these rewards or having to grind stats just to meet quest requirements. Always follow a quest guide that optimizes the order based on your current stats. The optimal quest order can save you 50-100 hours of grinding over the course of your account progression.

Mistake #2: Training Prayer too early (or too late). Prayer is an extremely powerful combat skill, but training it is very expensive if you use the fastest methods. Many beginners either try to train Prayer immediately (wasting GP they need for gear) or ignore it completely (missing out on巨额 combat bonuses). The optimal approach is to save your bones from Slayer tasks and use them at a Gilded Altar later when you have the GP for incenses and a house. Check our Prayer guide for the optimal training strategy.

Mistake #4: Not doing Farming runs. Farming is the most time-efficient skill in OSRS because it is time-gated rather than action-gated. Doing tree runs and herb runs every day takes only a few minutes but provides substantial profit and experience over time. Many beginners ignore Farming because the early levels are slow, but this is a huge mistake. Start Farming as early as possible, even if you can only afford low-level seeds. The compound growth of daily Farming runs is incredible.

Mistake #5: Ignoring Agility. Agility is often neglected by beginners because the experience rates are slow and the training is tedious. However, having high Agility (70+) reduces your run energy drain significantly and unlocks shortcuts that save enormous amounts of time. The Hallowed Sepulchre (Agility 72+) provides by far the fastest Agility experience and excellent loot. Train Agility consistently throughout your account progression, even if it is just 30-60 minutes per day. The time investment pays for itself many times over.

Mistake #6: Not using the Wiki. The OSRS Wiki is the single most valuable resource for any player. It contains exact experience rates, quest requirements, monster drop tables, and virtually everything you need to know about the game. Many beginners try to play "blind" to avoid spoilers, but OSRS is a complex game with 20+ years of content. Using the Wiki to look up information is not cheating — it is an essential part of playing efficiently. Make it a habit to check the Wiki before starting any new activity.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: How long does it take to max all 23 skills in OSRS as a beginner?

For a completely new player following efficient methods, maxing all 23 skills (reaching 99 in everything) typically takes 1,500 to 3,000 hours of gameplay. This assumes you are using the fastest methods and have the GP to fund production skills. If you are playing more casually and using slower but cheaper methods, it could take 5,000+ hours. The good news is that you don't need to max to enjoy the game — many players find the journey to 99 in a few favorite skills to be rewarding enough. Our skills progression guide can help you plan your journey efficiently.

Q2: Should I focus on one skill at a time or train multiple skills simultaneously?

The most efficient approach is usually to train multiple skills simultaneously through activities that train several skills at once. For example, Slayer trains all combat skills simultaneously, Wintertodt trains Firemaking and rewards other skills, and Farming can be done passively alongside other activities. However, for production skills like Crafting or Herblore, you will typically want to focus on them for a dedicated session because they require buying/collecting large quantities of materials. The key is to integrate your training so you are always making progress in multiple areas.

Q3: What is the best money-making method for a beginner with low stats?

For beginners with low stats (combat 1-50, no high-level skills), the best money-making methods are: Cowhide collection (collect cowhides and tan them in Al Kharid for ~1M GP/hour at higher efficiency), Flax picking and spinning (pick flax in Seers' Village, spin into bowstrings), Mining Iron Ore (especially in the Mining Guild at 60+ Mining), and Completing quests that provide GP rewards. As your stats improve, move on to Slayer and bossing for much higher profits. Check our money-making guide for methods appropriate to your current stats.

Q4: When should I start doing bossing content in OSRS?

You can start doing entry-level bossing content as soon as you have decent gear and stats. Barrows (combat 70+ recommended) is the first "real" boss content most players tackle. Giant Mole (combat 60+ can safespot) and King Black Dragon (combat 70+) are also good entry-level bosses. Before attempting any boss, always check the boss guides on our site to understand the mechanics and requirements. Do not rush into bossing — having the right gear and stats makes a huge difference in your success rate and profits.

Q5: Is it worth it to get membership as a brand new player?

Absolutely. While F2P OSRS is a valid way to play, P2P unlocks all 23 skills, hundreds of quests, and vastly more content. The most efficient path is to play F2P for a few days to learn the basics, then upgrade to P2P as soon as possible. With P2P, you gain access to Slayer (the best combat training method), Farming (essential for long-term profit), and countless other skills and content that make the game much more enjoyable. The monthly membership cost is very reasonable considering the amount of content you get access to.

Q6: How important is the account build (main, ironman, hardcore ironman) for a beginner?

For your first OSRS account, we highly recommend playing a main account (regular P2P account). Main accounts have access to the Grand Exchange, can receive help from other players, and can experience all content without the restrictions of ironman mode. Ironman accounts (where you cannot trade with other players or use the GE) are popular but significantly more challenging and time-consuming. Only choose ironman if you explicitly want that challenge. Your first account should be about learning the game, and main accounts are the best for that. Our new player guide has more information on choosing your account type.

Final Tips for Long-Term Success

As we conclude this comprehensive beginner training guide, here are some final tips to ensure your long-term success and enjoyment in OSRS. First, set realistic goals and celebrate your progress. OSRS is a marathon, not a sprint. The sense of accomplishment from reaching level 99 after hundreds of hours of training is unmatched in gaming. Second, join a clan. The OSRS community is one of the best in gaming, and having experienced players to answer your questions and chat with makes the game much more enjoyable. Third, don't be afraid to take breaks. OSRS is designed to be played at your own pace, and burnout is real. Taking a week or two off will not hurt your account.

Fourth, always be learning. OSRS has 20+ years of content, and there is always something new to discover. Watch YouTube guides, read the OSRS Wiki, and ask questions in your clan. The more you learn about the game's mechanics, the more efficient and enjoyable your playtime will be. Fifth, have a plan but stay flexible. The weekly training routes in this guide are a framework, not a rigid schedule. Adapt them based on your playstyle, interests, and in-game circumstances. If you find yourself really enjoying bossing, feel free to deviate from the plan to do more PvM. The best OSRS account is one that you enjoy playing.

Finally, remember that OSRS is a game, and games are meant to be fun. Efficiency is important, but not at the cost of enjoyment. If you find a particular training method boring, switch to something else. The beauty of OSRS is that there are multiple valid paths to every goal. Whether you want to be a sweaty efficiency scaper or a casual skiller who enjoys the scenery, there is a place for you in Gielinor. We hope this guide has given you the foundation you need to start your journey. Good luck, have fun, and we'll see you in the game!
