Crimson Desert launched on GeForce Now on day one — March 19, 2026 — making it one of the few AAA 2026 titles available for cloud streaming at launch. For players without a high-end gaming PC or console, or for Steam Deck owners who want a smoother experience than native hardware can deliver, GeForce Now is an excellent alternative. The service streams the PC version of the game from NVIDIA's data centres, meaning you get the full PC feature set — DLSS 4, ray tracing, ultra textures — streamed to virtually any device.

However, cloud gaming introduces its own challenges: streaming artefacts, input latency, bandwidth requirements, and GeForce Now-specific graphical issues like shimmering, colour banding, and occasional session instability. This guide covers everything you need to know to get the best possible Crimson Desert experience on GeForce Now, backed by community testing from r/CrimsonDesert, the NVIDIA GeForce Now forums, and our own streaming benchmarks.

Network Requirements

🌐 Minimum vs Recommended Internet Speeds

Minimum (Free Tier, 1080p/30): 15 Mbps download, <10ms jitter, <50ms ping to nearest GFN data centre. Playable but compression artefacts will be visible in foliage and fast motion.

Recommended (Priority, 1080p/60): 50 Mbps download, <5ms jitter, <30ms ping. This is where the experience becomes visually acceptable with minimal artefacts.

Optimal (Ultimate, 4K/60-120): 100 Mbps download (wired ethernet), <2ms jitter, <15ms ping. Fibre internet strongly recommended. 4K HDR streaming at 125 Mbps is demanding — Wi-Fi 6 or wired ethernet required.

Wi-Fi Warning: Crimson Desert on GeForce Now is sensitive to network instability. Wi-Fi (especially 2.4 GHz or congested 5 GHz) can cause periodic pixelation, micro-stutters, and audio desync. For the best experience, use a wired ethernet connection. If you must use Wi-Fi, ensure you are on a 5 GHz or 6 GHz band with minimal interference, and sit within 5 meters of the router. Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) is strongly recommended over Wi-Fi 5.

🎯 3. Shimmering & Ghosting Fix — DLSS, TAA & Settings That Work on GFN

Crimson Desert has well-documented ghosting (trailing artefacts behind moving objects) and shimmering (flickering pixels on edges) issues that affect all platforms, but they can be more pronounced on GeForce Now due to the double effect of in-game upscaling artefacts combined with streaming compression. This section provides fixes specifically for GFN users.

Root Causes of Shimmering on GeForce Now

Shimmering in Crimson Desert on GFN typically comes from three sources: (1) The game's temporal upscaling (DLSS/FSR) creating edge instability, (2) The streaming encoder's bitrate being insufficient for high-detail foliage scenes, and (3) The combination of TAA (Temporal Anti-Aliasing) with compressed video introducing flickering patterns.

Step-by-Step Shimmering Fix for GeForce Now

1

Switch to DLSS 4 (Not DLSS 4.5)

DLSS 4.5 has confirmed flickering issues on PC, and these artefacts are amplified by GFN's video compression. On Priority and Ultimate tiers (which have access to DLSS), use the NVIDIA App to force DLSS Model Preset to "Preset K" (DLSS 4). On GFN, you access the NVIDIA App through the in-stream overlay (Ctrl+G to open, then click "Graphics" → select Crimson Desert). Set "DLSS Model Preset" to "Custom" and "Super Resolution" to "Preset K." This eliminates the majority of shimmering caused by DLSS.

2

Disable TAA (or Use DLSS Native AA)

Crimson Desert's TAA implementation is particularly problematic with streaming compression. If you are on Ultimate tier, switch to DLSS Native AA mode (set upscaling to "DLSS" and quality to "Native AA"). This uses DLSS for anti-aliasing only, without upscaling, providing the cleanest image for streaming. On Priority tier, keep DLSS Quality mode but ensure TAA is set to the DLSS variant (not the separate TAA option in the anti-aliasing menu). On Free tier, use FSR 3.0 Quality mode and disable TAA completely — FSR handles AA internally.

3

Enable Ray Reconstruction (Ultimate & Priority)

NVIDIA Ray Reconstruction significantly reduces grass ghosting — one of the most distracting shimmering artefacts in Crimson Desert. Access the in-game Graphics menu → NVIDIA Ray Reconstruction → ON. Note the known bug: this causes rain to disappear visually. On GFN, accept the trade-off — non-ghosted grass is more important for general play than visible rain, especially since rain particle effects also suffer from compression artefacts during streaming.

4

Increase Streaming Bitrate to Maximum

In the GFN app (client side), set Bitrate to the maximum allowed by your tier: 75 Mbps for Priority, 125 Mbps for Ultimate. Higher bitrate reduces compression artefacts that exacerbate shimmering. If you are on a 1080p display, 50 Mbps is sufficient — the extra bitrate on 1080p has diminishing returns. On 4K displays, use the maximum available bitrate.

5

Set In-Game Sharpening Appropriately

Over-sharpening makes shimmering worse on streamed video. In the in-game Display settings, set Sharpening to 0.3-0.5 (on a 0-1 scale). Do not use the NVIDIA App's sharpening filter on top of in-game sharpening — use one or the other, not both. The in-game sharpener is sufficient. If using FSR, set sharpening to 0.5-0.7 (FSR tends to be softer than DLSS).

GFN Colour Issues: Some GeForce Now users have reported colour banding and shifting in Crimson Desert — colours appear washed out or have visible banding in sky gradients. This is caused by the streaming encoder's colour space conversion. Fix: In the GFN app, set "Color Space" to "sRGB" (not "Auto" or "HDR10" if you are not using HDR). On Ultimate with HDR, ensure both the GFN app and Windows HDR are properly configured — mismatched HDR settings cause the most severe colour issues. If colours shift when moving the camera, toggle the in-game HDR setting off and on again to reset the colour LUT.

⚡ 4. Input Latency Tips — Minimising Delay on Cloud Gaming

Input latency is the biggest concern for any action game streamed through cloud gaming, and Crimson Desert's combat — with its precise parry windows and combo timing — demands low latency. GeForce Now's Reflex integration helps, but there are additional steps you can take on your end.

Latency is Location-Dependent: Your physical distance to the nearest NVIDIA GFN data centre is the single biggest factor in input latency. Each data centre adds approximately 1ms of latency per 100km of fibre distance. Players in North America (closest to data centres) typically see 15-25ms network latency. Players in regions without nearby data centres may see 40-60ms network latency, making the total experience feel sluggish. Test your latency by running the GFN app's network test (Settings → Network → Test Network).

10 Tips to Minimise Input Latency on GFN

  • Use a wired controller: Wireless controllers (Bluetooth) add 8-15ms of input lag before the cloud even enters the equation. A wired USB connection eliminates this.
  • Enable NVIDIA Reflex: In the in-game settings, ensure NVIDIA Reflex is set to "On" (not "Off" or "On+Boost"). Reflex reduces render latency on the server-side GPU by synchronising the CPU and GPU frame pacing.
  • Use Game Mode on your local device: On PC, enable Windows Game Mode. On Mac, enable Low Latency Mode in the GFN app. On Shield TV, ensure Game Mode is active on the TV itself.
  • Reduce streaming resolution: Lowering the streaming resolution from 4K to 1440p or 1080p reduces the server-side rendering time and the decoding time on your local device. If you are latency-sensitive, 1080p/120 FPS streaming on Ultimate feels more responsive than 4K/60 FPS.
  • Disable VSync in GFN app: The GFN app's VSync option (under Settings → Streaming) adds 1-2 frames of latency. Disable it and rely on in-game VSync or G-Sync/FreeSync on your display.
  • Close bandwidth-heavy applications: Streaming video, large downloads, or video calls on the same network increase jitter and packet loss, which translates to uneven input response. Use your router's QoS settings to prioritise GFN traffic if possible.
  • Use ethernet, not Wi-Fi: This cannot be overstated. Every Wi-Fi hop adds 2-5ms of variable latency. A wired connection provides consistent sub-1ms network latency.
  • Choose the right GFN server region: In the GFN app settings, manually select the server region closest to you. Auto-select sometimes chooses a suboptimal server. In North America, US West (California) and US East (Virginia) are the primary options — choose the one physically closer to you.
  • Play during off-peak hours: GeForce Now server load varies by time of day. Sessions started during peak evening hours (7-10 PM local time) may experience slightly higher latency due to server contention. Morning and afternoon sessions typically have the lowest latency.
  • Consider a 120 FPS stream on Ultimate: Even if your display is 60 Hz, streaming at 120 FPS and letting the GFN app down-sample to 60 FPS can reduce perceived latency because the server renders frames twice as frequently, giving you a more recent frame to display. Test this — the effect varies by game and connection quality.

💰 5. Free vs Priority vs Ultimate — Which Tier Should You Choose?

Choosing the right GeForce Now tier for Crimson Desert depends on your budget, display setup, and tolerance for compromises. Here is our tier-by-tier recommendation.

Free Tier — Only If Necessary

Free ($0/month)

Verdict: Playable but compromised. The 1-hour session limit is the biggest problem — you cannot complete a major quest chain or exploration run without the session ending. The GTX 1060-class GPU runs Crimson Desert at Low-Medium settings with FSR at 1080p, delivering 30-45 FPS. No ray tracing, no DLSS. The image quality is roughly equivalent to Xbox Series S Quality mode, but streamed. Only use Free tier to test if Crimson Desert runs well on your network before subscribing. For actual gameplay, Priority or Ultimate is strongly recommended.

Priority Tier — The Sweet Spot

Priority ($9.99/month)

Verdict: The best value for most players. The RTX 2080-class GPU handles Crimson Desert at High settings, 1080p/60 FPS with DLSS 4 Quality and Low RT. The 6-hour session limit covers virtually any play session. Image quality matches or exceeds PS5 and Xbox Series X. The main limitation is 1080p-only output — if you have a 4K monitor, you are not getting full resolution. But at $10/month, this is significantly cheaper than buying a $500 console or $1000+ gaming PC. Recommended for 1080p display owners.

Ultimate Tier — The Premium Experience

Ultimate ($19.99/month)

Verdict: The definitive cloud gaming experience for Crimson Desert. The RTX 4080-class GPU runs the game at Ultra settings, 4K/60-120 FPS with DLSS 4 Quality + Frame Gen, Cinematic ray tracing, and HDR streaming. This surpasses PS5 Pro visual quality and rivals a local PC with an RTX 4070 Ti Super. The 8-hour session limit is generous, and high-priority queue access means you rarely wait to play. HDR streaming is a genuine game-changer for Crimson Desert's vibrant world. Recommended for 4K display owners, HDR enthusiasts, and anyone who wants the absolute best cloud gaming experience.

Cost Comparison: Ultimate tier ($19.99/month × 12 = $240/year) over three years costs $720. A comparable local PC (RTX 4070 Ti Super build) costs approximately $1,800-2,200. If you do not mind subscription pricing and have good internet, Ultimate is significantly cheaper over the short to medium term. The trade-off is dependency on internet quality and NVIDIA's service availability.

🔧 6. Known Issues & Workarounds — Colour Problems, Crashes & Session Limits

Issue 1: Colour Banding and Shifting

1

Fix: Colour Space Configuration

Colour banding (visible stripes in sky gradients) and colour shifting (colours changing when the camera moves) are reported by GFN users. Fix: In GFN app → Settings → Streaming → Color Space → Set to "sRGB" (not Auto). If using HDR on Ultimate, ensure your display is properly HDR-calibrated in Windows before launching the game. Toggle the in-game HDR setting off and back on if colours appear wrong after launching. On NVIDIA Shield TV, ensure the display output is set to "RGB" (not "YCbCr") for the most accurate colours.

Issue 2: Session Timeout During Long Play

2

Fix: Plan Around Session Limits

Free tier sessions end after 1 hour, Priority after 6 hours, Ultimate after 8 hours. When your session is about to end, GeForce Now shows a 5-minute warning. Save your game immediately. On Free tier, you can immediately start a new session after a session ends — but you may have to wait in a queue. On Priority and Ultimate, new sessions start instantly with no queue. Note that some progression may be lost if the session ends during an autosave — save manually before the warning timer expires.

Issue 3: Micro-Stuttering Every 30-60 Seconds

3

Fix: Enable VSync in Game + Disable in GFN App

Micro-stuttering on GFN is often caused by the interaction between the game's frame pacing and the streaming encoder's frame capture. The fix: Enable V-Sync in the in-game settings (Options → Graphics → VSync → ON), and disable V-Sync in the GFN app (Settings → Streaming → VSync → OFF). This ensures the game produces consistently-timed frames that the encoder can capture cleanly. Also ensure "Adaptive Bitrate" in the GFN app is ON — it will reduce quality slightly during network fluctuations rather than stuttering.

Issue 4: Audio Desync

4

Fix: Reset Audio Device + Lower Bitrate

Audio desync (sound lagging behind video) is usually a local device issue rather than a GFN server issue. Fix: Close the GFN app completely → Disconnect and reconnect your audio device (headphones/speakers) → Re-launch GFN → Restart Crimson Desert. If the issue persists, lower the streaming bitrate by 25% (e.g., from 75 to 50 Mbps) — this reduces the encoder's workload and can resolve audio/video sync issues. On PC, ensure your audio drivers are up to date.

Issue 5: Game Crashes to GFN Desktop

5

Fix: Verify Game Files via Steam on GFN

Crimson Desert may crash back to the GFN virtual desktop (showing a Windows desktop with the Steam interface). This is usually caused by corrupted game files on the server-side installation. Fix: In the GFN virtual desktop, open Steam → Library → Right-click Crimson Desert → Properties → Local Files → Verify Integrity of Game Files. This downloads any corrupted files and typically resolves crash issues. Note that this fix only applies during your current GFN session — the next session may or may not use the same server installation, so you may need to repeat this.

Issue 6: Overlay Not Working (Ctrl+G)

6

Fix: Update GFN App + Check Shortcut Conflicts

The GeForce Now in-game overlay (Ctrl+G) can fail to open on some devices. Fix: Update the GFN app to version 2.0.83 or newer (Settings → About → Check for Updates). This version added customizable overlay shortcuts. If the overlay still does not work, check for conflicting shortcuts — some applications (Discord, NVIDIA ShadowPlay, Windows Game Bar) may be intercepting Ctrl+G. Either change the GFN overlay shortcut (Settings → Overlay → Change Shortcut) or disable the conflicting application's shortcut.

❓ FAQ — GeForce Now Questions Answered

Does Crimson Desert support cross-save between PC and GeForce Now?

Yes. GeForce Now runs the Steam version of Crimson Desert, and Steam Cloud saves sync automatically between your local PC and GFN sessions. You can play on your local PC at home, then continue on GFN via a laptop or Shield TV while travelling. Ensure Steam Cloud is enabled for Crimson Desert in your Steam library settings. Save files are approximately 50-100MB, so syncing may take a few seconds when switching platforms.

Can I use mods on GeForce Now with Crimson Desert?

No. GeForce Now runs a clean installation of the game on virtualized servers. You cannot install mods, custom DLLs, or modified configuration files. Any mod that requires file replacement or injection will not work. This includes the FSR 4 mod, DLSS Swapper, and Lossless Scaling. If mods are important to you, you need to play on a local PC.

Is Crimson Desert on GeForce Now available in my region?

GeForce Now is available in over 100 countries, but Crimson Desert's availability on the service depends on regional licensing. The game is available on GFN in North America, Europe, South Korea, Japan, Australia, and most of Southeast Asia. Some regions (notably mainland China) do not have GFN data centres or may have restricted game availability. Check the GeForce Now website for your region's game library.

Does GeForce Now support keyboard and mouse for Crimson Desert?

Yes, fully. GeForce Now passes through keyboard and mouse input from your local device to the cloud server. On PC and Mac, your local KB+M works directly. On Shield TV, you can connect a wireless KB+M via USB. On mobile devices, touch controls are available but not recommended for Crimson Desert's complex combat — use a controller or KB+M instead.

Which GeForce Now tier do I need for 1440p 60 FPS?

Only the Ultimate tier supports 1440p streaming. Priority and Free tiers are capped at 1080p output, even if you select a higher resolution in the in-game settings (the GFN app will downscale to 1080p for streaming). If you have a 1440p monitor, Ultimate tier is the minimum requirement for native-resolution streaming.

Will my GeForce Now session save my in-game settings?

In-game graphics settings should persist across sessions because they are stored in the cloud save (part of the user profile). However, some users report that settings reset between sessions on GFN. To save your settings reliably, after configuring them, exit the game normally (through the in-game menu), wait for Steam Cloud to sync, and then close the GFN session. Settings changed during a session that ends abruptly (server timeout, crash) may not be saved.

Can I play Crimson Desert on GeForce Now via Steam Deck?

Yes — and it is actually recommended over native Steam Deck play for better visual quality and frame rates. Install the GeForce Now app in Desktop Mode from the Discover store, or add it as a non-Steam game in Game Mode. You can then stream Crimson Desert at 1080p/60 FPS (Priority) or 4K/60 FPS (Ultimate). The input latency on Steam Deck via Wi-Fi is higher than on a wired connection, but the visual quality improvement over native play is dramatic. See our Steam Deck guide for a comparison of native vs streaming on Deck.

Does Crimson Desert support HDR on GeForce Now?

HDR streaming is exclusive to the Ultimate tier. It requires an HDR-capable display, a device that supports HDR streaming (PC with Windows HDR enabled, Shield TV Pro, or supported smart TVs), and the AV1 or HEVC codec. Enable HDR in the GFN app (Settings → Streaming → HDR → ON) and then calibrate HDR in Crimson Desert's in-game settings. The HDR stream quality is excellent on Ultimate — colour volume and brightness are well-preserved by NVIDIA's encoder.
