Crimson Desert Shadow Flicker & Vegetation Jitter Fix

If the grass dances, the leaves shimmer, and shadows pop in and out as you move, you are not imagining it. This guide shows the exact graphics settings — shadow quality, contact shadows, foliage density, anti-aliasing, and draw distance — that calm the flicker on PS5, PS5 Pro, and PC.

🟢 Novice Graphics Fix · 7 min read · Updated July 2026

⏱️ Quick Summary — 30-Second Read

  • 📌 Shadow flicker is usually caused by high shadow quality + long draw distance fighting for GPU headroom.
  • 📌 Vegetation jitter comes from dense foliage plus weak anti-aliasing shimmering on thin leaves.
  • 📌 Set Shadow Quality to Medium and turn Contact Shadows off for the biggest stability gain.
  • 📌 Drop Foliage Density to Medium and use TAA or FSR AA instead of off/none.
  • 📌 Lower Draw Distance one notch to shrink the amount of geometry the engine re-renders each frame.

🌿 1. What Causes Shadow Flicker & Vegetation Jitter

Crimson Desert renders a huge, lush open world. That beauty has a cost: thousands of shadow-casting objects and dense vegetation all compete for the same GPU budget every frame. When the engine cannot keep up, two visual glitches appear.

🔦 Shadow Flicker

Shadows "pop," crawl, or strobe along edges as the camera moves. This happens when shadow resolution is too high for the distance it covers — the shadow map runs out of texels, so tiny details alias and flicker. Long draw distance makes it worse because more objects share the same shadow map.

🌾 Vegetation Jitter

Grass, leaves, and branches shimmer or "crawl" when you move. This is geometry aliasing: thin, high-frequency surfaces (blades of grass) are not sampled smoothly, especially with weak or disabled anti-aliasing. High foliage density stacks millions of these surfaces on screen at once.

💡 Good News

Neither problem is a hardware fault. Both are settings-driven, so you can fix them entirely in the graphics menu in about two minutes.

🎚️ 2. Shadow Quality: The #1 Fix

Shadow Quality controls the resolution of the shadow map. Ultra / High looks crisp up close but forces the engine to re-render a massive shadow map every frame, and when the GPU is busy the map samples inconsistently — that is your flicker.

① Set Shadow Quality to Medium

Medium is the sweet spot. It keeps shadows readable while cutting the per-frame shadow cost dramatically, which is what stops the popping. On weaker GPUs, Low is fine and removes flicker almost entirely.

② Avoid "Ultra" Unless You Have Headroom

If you are on a base PS5 or a mid-range PC GPU, Ultra shadows are the single most common cause of shadow strobing in towns and forests. Drop it before touching anything else.

⚠️ Test One Change at a Time

Change Shadow Quality, then walk into a forest and look at the ground. If flicker remains, move to the next setting. This isolates what actually helps on your hardware.

🟦 3. Contact Shadows: Disable for Stability

Contact Shadows add small, soft shadows where objects meet the ground (great for grounding grass and rocks). But they are computed separately and are a frequent source of micro-strobing on vegetation, because thousands of grass blades each try to cast a tiny contact shadow.

① Turn Contact Shadows Off

Disabling them removes a large chunk of vegetation flicker with almost no visible loss in open-world scenes. This is the highest-impact toggle after Shadow Quality.

② If You Must Keep Them, Lower Resolution

Some builds let you set contact shadow resolution. Keep it at Low. Never pair High contact shadows with High foliage density — that combination is the classic jitter recipe.

💡 Why This Works

Contact shadows are screen-space effects recalculated every frame. Cutting them reduces the number of shadow passes the GPU must finish in your frame budget — fewer passes, fewer flickers.

🌳 4. Foliage Density: Calm the Canopy

Foliage Density decides how many grass blades, bushes, and leaves are drawn. At High / Ultra, the engine pushes millions of thin, high-frequency surfaces that anti-aliasing struggles to sample — producing the crawling, shimmering canopy.

① Drop Foliage Density to Medium

Medium visibly reduces the number of grass instances while still looking lush. The shimmer drops because there is simply less fine geometry to alias.

② On Weak Hardware, Use Low

Low foliage is the most reliable way to kill vegetation jitter on base PS5 or older PCs. You lose some ground cover, but the world stops crawling.

⚠️ Do Not Crank Foliage With Weak AA

Foliage Density and Anti-Aliasing interact. High foliage + off/weak AA = guaranteed shimmer. Lower foliage first, then fix AA (next section).

🔍 5. Anti-Aliasing: Stop the Shimmer

Anti-aliasing (AA) is what smooths jagged edges and, crucially, reduces the shimmer on thin vegetation. Running with AA off is the second-biggest cause of grass jitter after high foliage density.

① Use TAA or FSR AA (Not Off)

Temporal Anti-Aliasing (TAA) samples across frames, which is exactly what thin grass needs to stop crawling. FSR's AA mode also helps. Avoid "None" unless your GPU is very weak.

② If TAA Looks Too Soft, Use FSR Quality + AA

Some players find TAA blurs distant detail. On PC, pair FSR at "Quality" with its AA on. On PS5, TAA is usually the best balance of sharpness and stability.

💡 AA vs FPS

TAA is cheaper than you think and prevents the most annoying jitter. The FPS you "save" by disabling AA is not worth the shimmer it creates.

📏 6. Draw Distance: Less Geometry, Less Flicker

Draw Distance controls how far the engine renders objects, including shadows and foliage. Longer distance means more objects share the same shadow map and more vegetation is visible — both amplify flicker.

① Lower Draw Distance One Notch

Moving from Ultra to High (or High to Medium) shrinks the rendered world slightly but cuts the shadow-map load and foliage count meaningfully. This is a quiet hero setting for flicker.

② Keep It Consistent With Shadow Quality

If shadows are Medium but draw distance is Ultra, you still over-load the shadow map with distant objects. Match them: Medium shadows + Medium-High draw distance.

🔧 The 5-Setting Combo

The reliable anti-flicker preset is: Shadow Quality Medium · Contact Shadows Off · Foliage Density Medium · AA TAA/FSR · Draw Distance one step down. Apply all five together for a clean, stable image.

🎮 8. Console vs PC Notes

The fixes above work on both platforms, but the menus differ slightly.

🕹️ PS5 / PS5 Pro

Open Settings → Graphics. Use Performance Mode (not Resolution Mode) for extra headroom, then apply the five-setting combo. Consoles cannot roll back patches, so settings are your only lever — but they fully fix the flicker for most players.

🖥️ PC

You have finer control and can also update your GPU driver for better shadow handling. If flicker persists after settings, a driver update (or a clean driver reinstall) often resolves residual shadow-map artifacts.

⚠️ Mods Are Not the Answer

Do not install "anti-flicker" mods or config hacks. They risk your save data and account, and the in-game settings already solve this cleanly.

❓ 9. FAQs

Will lowering these settings make the game look bad?

Minimally. Shadow Quality Medium and Foliage Medium still look great in motion. The flicker you remove is far more distracting than the tiny detail you trade away.

Why does grass shimmer even when I am standing still?

That is usually weak or disabled anti-aliasing plus dense foliage. Turn AA on (TAA/FSR) and drop Foliage Density — the standing-still shimmer should disappear.

Is shadow flicker a 1.13 regression like the FPS drop?

Not exactly. Flicker is settings-driven and present on most builds; the 1.13 FPS regression is separate. Fixing settings helps both, but the FPS regression also needs the patch notes covered in our performance guide.

Do I need to restart the game after changing these?

Most take effect instantly. Shadow and foliage changes sometimes need you to move to a new area (or a quick menu close/reopen) to fully refresh. A full restart is never required.

My GPU is high-end but I still see flicker — why?

Ultra shadow quality with Ultra draw distance can still over-load even strong GPUs in dense forests. Drop both one notch; the flicker is about shadow-map coverage, not raw power.
